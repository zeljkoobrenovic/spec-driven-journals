> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization will adopt a weekly habit of making, recording, and sharing small engineering improvements. Each team should look for one useful "1%" improvement: small enough to complete quickly, concrete enough to notice, and shareable enough for others to copy or adapt. The habit exists to create visible continuous progress and cumulative long-term impact.

## Decision

Adopt **One Percent Improvements** as a lightweight weekly engineering habit.

The practice is simple:

1. Each team identifies one small improvement during the week.
2. The team makes the improvement or proves why it needs a follow-up.
3. The team shares a short note where other teams can see it.
4. Repeated patterns are connected into standards, principles, records, or larger improvement work.

"One percent" is not a measurement target. It is a reminder to look for improvements small enough to keep moving.

## How to Read This Decision

This decision asks teams to build a habit, not a reporting programme.

| It is | It is not |
| --- | --- |
| A weekly prompt to make one small thing better. | A quota for productivity theatre. |
| A way to make progress visible. | A replacement for roadmap work. |
| A source of morale through agency and motion. | A substitute for fixing systemic blockers. |
| A way to let useful local practices spread. | A central approval process. |
| A feed of signals for guilds, curators, and the AAF. | A demand that every improvement become a formal record. |

The habit works when teams feel more agency at the end of the week than they had at the start.

## Rationale

Large improvements matter, but they are expensive to start and easy to postpone. Minor improvements are different. They fit between normal work, create a short feedback loop, and help teams experience progress even when larger outcomes take months.

The theory of minor improvements has two claims:

| Claim | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| **Visible progress improves morale.** | People are more willing to improve a system when they can see recent evidence that improvement is possible. |
| **Small changes compound.** | A single cleanup may be modest. Hundreds of shared cleanups across teams change the engineering environment. |

The cumulative effect is not only mathematical. It is cultural. Teams that repeatedly improve their tools, records, tests, dashboards, onboarding paths, and operational habits start to see improvement as part of the work rather than as a special initiative.

If a capability could literally improve by one percent every week, the annual compounding effect would be large. Most engineering improvements do not measure that cleanly. The practical point is simpler: small friction reductions accumulate, and the morale created by visible progress makes the next improvement easier to start.

## Weekly Process

The process should fit into existing team routines.

| Step | Question | Output |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Notice | What slowed us down, confused us, or created avoidable friction this week? | Candidate improvement. |
| Choose | What can we improve in less than a few hours? | One focused action. |
| Do | Can we ship the improvement now, or at least reduce uncertainty? | Change, note, or follow-up. |
| Share | What changed and why might others care? | Short weekly note. |
| Connect | Is this a repeated pattern or cross-team issue? | Link, record, guild topic, or AAF topic. |

The weekly note should be short:

| Field | Example |
| --- | --- |
| **Improvement** | Added a missing runbook link to the service dashboard. |
| **Why it helped** | On-call engineers no longer need to search three places during alerts. |
| **Can others copy it?** | Yes: dashboard panels should link directly to the runbook and owner channel. |
| **Follow-up** | Backend Guild to check whether other tier-one services have the same gap. |

If a team cannot find anything small, that is a useful signal. It may mean the team is overloaded, the work is too fragmented, or the improvement opportunities are too large for a weekly habit and need explicit planning.

## What Counts as a Minor Improvement

A good one percent improvement is small, real, and repeatable.

| Area | Good examples |
| --- | --- |
| Code | Delete dead code, remove a dead feature flag, simplify a confusing helper, add a missing test around a brittle path. |
| Operations | Delete unused infrastructure resources, add an actionable alert link, clarify a dashboard title, fix a runbook step discovered during support. |
| Delivery | Remove a manual release step, improve a flaky local command, document a recurring review checklist. |
| Knowledge | Archive an inactive source code repository, add a missing cross-link, write a short decision note, clarify ownership in a README. |
| Onboarding | Improve the first-run path, name the common setup failure, add one screenshot where people get stuck. |
| Collaboration | Close an inactive Slack channel, replace a vague hand-off with a clear owner, expectation, and next action. |

The improvement should leave the environment better than it was. A meeting about improvement does not count unless it produces a change, decision, or sharper follow-up.

## Relationship to Technical Debt

Some one percent improvements are technical-debt removal: deleting dead code, archiving inactive repositories, removing unused infrastructure, simplifying brittle paths, or closing obsolete collaboration spaces.

But the habit is broader than formal technical-debt management. Teams should make these improvements even when nobody has labelled the friction as debt yet. A confusing script, stale dashboard, unclear owner, unused resource, or abandoned channel may be too small to enter a debt register, but it still taxes attention every week.

The best use of the habit is preventive. Remove small sources of friction before they become visible technical debt, incident contributors, onboarding traps, or recurring morale drains.

## Sharing the Improvement

Sharing is part of the work because local improvements often solve shared problems.

A useful share note:

* names the specific friction;
* describes the small change;
* links the artifact if there is one;
* says whether another team can copy it;
* names a follow-up only when one is needed.

Teams should avoid polished success stories. The best notes are short, concrete, and honest. "We found this annoying and removed it" is more useful than a ceremonial update.

## From Local Notes to Durable Records

Most weekly improvements stay local. Some become signals.

| Signal | Next step |
| --- | --- |
| Several teams fix the same documentation gap. | Curators improve the relevant record structure. |
| Several teams patch around the same platform limitation. | Bring the pattern to the relevant guild. |
| A small improvement reveals a standards gap. | Draft or update a foundation, principle, or decision record. |
| A recurring issue has architecture-wide consequences. | Bring it to [[architecture-advisory-forum]]. |
| A local fix becomes generally reusable. | Share the template, script, checklist, or example. |

The goal is not to formalise everything. The goal is to notice when local progress is telling the organisation something.

## What This Means for Teams

* Treat improvement as part of normal engineering work.
* Keep the improvement small enough to finish.
* Prefer concrete changes over broad intentions.
* Share useful notes even when the change feels modest.
* Copy and adapt improvements from other teams.
* Escalate patterns, not every individual note.
* Protect the habit from becoming a metric or ceremony.

Managers and tech leads should make room for the practice by asking, "What did we make easier this week?" and by valuing unglamorous work that reduces future friction.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Improvement quota.** Teams invent low-value activity because someone expects a weekly number.
* **Heroic cleanup.** The "minor" improvement quietly becomes a large unplanned project.
* **Private progress.** Teams improve locally but do not share, so others repeat the same discovery.
* **Cosmetic motion.** Notes celebrate activity that did not change the system, workflow, or understanding.
* **Debt register dependency.** Teams wait for a formal technical-debt label before removing obvious small friction.
* **Blame through visibility.** Shared notes are used to compare teams rather than spread learning.
* **Avoiding systemic work.** Minor improvements are used to dodge the larger investment a recurring problem needs.

## Success Indicators

The habit is working when:

* teams can name recent improvements without searching;
* small irritations disappear before they become accepted background noise;
* improvement notes are copied across teams;
* repeated notes reveal standards, tooling, or architecture patterns;
* people see that they have permission to improve the environment around them;
* the organisation has fewer "everyone knows this is bad" problems that never get touched.

## Scope and Revisiting

This decision applies to engineering teams and communities of practice. It covers small improvements to code, delivery, operations, documentation, collaboration, and local engineering systems.

It does not replace planned platform work, incident remediation, roadmap commitments, architectural decision-making, or investment in systemic problems. When the same minor improvement keeps recurring, treat it as evidence that the system needs a larger change.

Revisit this record after one quarter of use. Look for whether the habit is still lightweight, whether people find the shared notes useful, and whether repeated patterns are being connected to durable records or action.

## Appendix: Vocabulary

| Term | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| **One percent improvement** | A small improvement that makes the engineering environment measurably or noticeably better. |
| **Minor improvement** | A change small enough to complete quickly but real enough to reduce future friction. |
| **Weekly note** | The short shared description of what changed, why it helped, and whether others can copy it. |
| **Pattern** | Repeated minor improvements that point to a wider opportunity, gap, or systemic problem. |
| **Compounding** | The cumulative effect of many small improvements reinforcing each other over time. |
| **Technical debt** | A design, code, infrastructure, process, or knowledge gap that creates future cost unless it is repaid or removed. |
