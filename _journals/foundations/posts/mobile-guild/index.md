> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization will form a **Mobile Guild** as a standing community of practice for **mobile** engineers across teams, brands, and domains. The guild is the organisation-wide mobile consortium that enables and empowers teams around standards to ensure the long-term growth of the mobile ecosystem - native iOS and Android, cross-platform stacks where used, and mobile-specific shells. It focuses on mobile challenges first and uses them as an entry point to end-to-end concerns (backend, infrastructure, ...) where it makes sense. The guild is **advisory and contributory**, not a governance body: it runs bi-weekly knowledge sharing, experimentation-first work in its own GitHub area, transparent recorded sessions, working groups, and coordinated external visibility. **Web frontend is explicitly out of scope**; a separate Frontend Guild handles it. The guild is provisioned with a dedicated Slack channel, source code repositories, and an AWS account (or a dedicated space in an existing account).

## Decision

Establish a **Mobile Guild** at Organization as a cross-team community of practice for **mobile** work. Concretely:

* **Purpose**: act as the organisation-wide mobile consortium that enables and empowers teams around standards; resolve the open questions that no single team can settle alone (mobile design system, app architecture, modularisation, release and store delivery, mobile-to-backend communication, state management, native ↔ cross-platform tradeoffs, observability on device).
* **Scope**: **mobile**. Native iOS, native Android, cross-platform stacks (e.g., Kotlin Multiplatform, React Native, Flutter) where Organization uses them, and mobile-specific shells. Web frontend is out of scope; a separate Frontend Guild owns it. Where a topic genuinely spans both, the guilds coordinate explicitly rather than absorbing each other's scope.
* **Composition**: open to every Aviver, temporarily or permanently. A small core organising group keeps the cadence honest and the agenda alive.
* **Mandate**: bi-weekly knowledge sharing; guild sessions at Architecture Summits and Talks; experimentation-first work documented in the guild's GitHub area; transparent, recorded sessions; coaching; ad-hoc working groups; feedback to the architecture group and Chief Architect; the right to propose new recommendations and decisions; coordinated external visibility.
* **Resources**: a dedicated Slack channel, source code repositories under a guild-owned area, and an AWS account (or a dedicated space inside an existing account) for shared experiments, reference implementations, and golden-path code.

This foundation **does not** create a decision-making board, **does not** transfer ownership of team-owned mobile apps to the guild, and **does not** make guild participation a prerequisite for contributing to mobile work at Organization.

## How to Read This Decision

Several ideas in this record are easy to confuse with traditional centralised teams. The distinctions matter:

| Idea | What it means here | What it does **not** mean |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Guild** | A standing community of practice for mobile engineers. | A team that owns delivery. |
| **Mobile consortium** | A cross-team forum for shared mobile standards and practice. | A standards body that mandates compliance. |
| **Standards** | Opinionated, well-supported defaults teams adopt by choice. | A binding policy that bans alternatives. |
| **Mandate** | A clear set of activities the guild is empowered to run. | Authority to approve or block other teams' decisions. |
| **Experimentation-first** | Concrete prototypes and reference implementations in the guild's GitHub area. | Production ownership of those experiments. |
| **Feedback to architecture** | A standing practitioner channel to the architecture group and Chief Architect. | A veto over architecture decisions. |

If you have worked in organisations where a mobile guild gradually became a closed standards body or a shadow architecture board, the Mobile Guild is deliberately different. It is a learning and coordination structure, not a governance structure. The [[frontend-guild]] and [[backend-guild]] are shaped on the same principles.

## One-Minute Mental Model

Think of the Mobile Guild as the **standing community where mobile engineers meet, build, and shape practice across teams**:

1. Teams keep ownership of their mobile apps.
2. The guild proposes and refines the standards, reference implementations, and golden paths the teams can adopt.
3. The guild's bi-weekly sessions surface lessons learned across the organisation and are recorded for the rest of the company.
4. The guild proposes recommendations and decisions; the architecture group and the [[architecture-advisory-forum]] handle the actual decision flow.
5. The guild grows experts and gives them a coordinated platform to represent Organization externally.

The guild is succeeding when mobile engineers across brands feel they belong to one community and when shared standards and reference implementations are visibly maintained and trusted. It is failing when it becomes a closed club, a parallel decision body, or quietly absorbs web concerns it is not set up to handle.

## Purpose (Why)

A lot of open questions in Organization's mobile work cannot be answered well by a single team. The Mobile Guild exists to give those questions a community and a place to work. The standing topics:

* **Mobile design system** - shared visual and interaction language, native component libraries (iOS, Android), and adoption paths across brands.
* **App architecture and modularisation** - patterns for structuring large mobile codebases, modular builds, feature flags, and ownership boundaries.
* **Native vs cross-platform strategy** - where Organization uses native (Swift, Kotlin) and where it uses cross-platform stacks (KMP, React Native, Flutter), and the tradeoffs that drive those choices.
* **Release and store delivery** - app store release cadence, rollout strategies, phased releases, store metadata, review-process handling, and crash-rate gates.
* **Communication between mobile clients and backend services** - API shapes, BFFs, GraphQL/REST, contract testing, offline-first patterns, and the boundary with the [[backend-guild]].
* **State management and offline** - shared and team-local patterns for managing UI state, server state, persistence, and offline behaviour.
* **Device APIs and platform integrations** - permissions, push, location, camera, biometrics, deep links, and the platform-specific surface area.
* **Observability on device** - crash reporting, performance, ANRs, battery, network behaviour, and the feedback loop into engineering.
* **Mobile CI/CD and signing** - build pipelines, signing, provisioning, artefact distribution, and beta channels.

The guild **focuses on mobile challenges first and uses them as an entry point to solve end-to-end challenges (backend, infrastructure, ...) where it makes sense**. The guild does not pretend to be a backend or infra guild; it surfaces end-to-end concerns and partners with the relevant guilds.

These topics are not exhaustive. The criterion for adding a topic is whether it is a *mobile* concern that benefits from being worked on across teams rather than within a single team.

## Mandate (How)

The guild operates through two complementary streams of work.

### Internal knowledge sharing

* **Bi-weekly sharing of lessons learned** - a recurring, predictable session where teams bring incidents, patterns, postmortems, and successes for the guild to discuss.
* **Architecture Summits and Talks** - the guild organises and runs guild-specific sessions during Organization's Architecture Summits and at relevant Talks.
* **Experimentation-first** - the guild's GitHub area is the primary working surface. Topics are explored through concrete prototypes, reference implementations, and shareable artifacts that teams can pick up directly.
* **Transparency** - all sessions are documented and recorded by default. The recordings and notes are accessible to the rest of the organisation, not just the attendees.
* **Connections and trust** - the guild deliberately builds connections and trust across the organisation around its main competency, so practitioners know who to ask and feel safe doing so.
* **Coaching and mutual help** - members coach each other, review designs, and pair on hard problems.
* **Connection with CoEs** - the guild maintains an explicit relationship with relevant Centres of Excellence so that CoE work and guild work reinforce rather than duplicate each other.
* **Synergies with other architecture guilds** - the guild coordinates with adjacent guilds (distributed systems design, security, data, [[backend-guild]], [[frontend-guild]]) on topics that cross boundaries.
* **Ad-hoc working groups** - the guild may spin up short, focused working groups on shared topics (e.g., a Kotlin/Swift version upgrade, a modularisation migration, a shared analytics SDK).
* **Feedback to architecture and Chief Architect** - the guild provides a standing practitioner channel on architecture recommendations and decisions.
* **Influence on strategic decisions** - the guild has a recognised seat in shaping key strategic tech decisions that affect mobile.
* **Proactive proposals** - the guild does not only react; it proactively proposes new recommendations and decisions where it sees a need. It is **impact-driven**.

### Coordinated thought leadership

* **Growing experts** - the guild grows world-class experts in specialised mobile domains by giving them a community, peers, and time to deepen.
* **Local events** - the guild coordinates organising local events (meetups, workshops, internal conferences) on mobile topics.
* **External visibility** - public blogging, external conference talks (iOS, Android, cross-platform conferences), and open-source contributions are promoted and coordinated through the guild so Organization presents a coherent voice externally.

Detailed internal ways of working - meeting cadence templates, working-group lifecycle, summit session contracts, external-talk approval flow - live in the guild's own working document (linked from the resources section below) and are owned by the guild itself.

## Composition (Who)

**Everyone is welcome to join the guild community, temporarily or permanently.** Mobile engineers (iOS, Android, cross-platform) across all teams, brands, and domains are the primary audience, but the door is genuinely open: designers, product engineers working on the mobile boundary, platform engineers, QA engineers with mobile focus, and anyone whose work touches the mobile layer can participate.

A small core organising group, led from or with the architecture group, keeps the cadence honest and the agenda alive. The core group is named, rotates, and is replaceable; it is not a closed steering committee.

The breadth of contributors is itself a leading indicator. A guild dominated by one platform (only iOS or only Android), one brand, or one team is not yet succeeding.

## Studies (What)

The guild aims to be **impact-driven** and prioritises its topics based on the needs of actor teams. Standing artefacts the guild maintains:

| Artefact | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| **[MG] Studies** | Time-boxed investigations of a topic the guild has chosen to look into. Output is a shareable write-up plus, where useful, a working prototype. |
| **[MG] Meeting minutes** | Lightweight record of bi-weekly sessions, decisions taken, follow-ups owned. Sessions are also recorded. |
| **[MG] ADRs** | The guild's own ADRs, recording decisions about its scope, recommended standards, and reference implementations. |
| **[MG] Resources** | Curated links, references, and reusable materials that the guild stands behind. |
| **[MG] Experiments** | Concrete prototypes and reference implementations in the guild's GitHub area, kept distinct from production-owned code. |

The `[MG]` prefix is a deliberate signal: it marks artefacts as guild outputs, distinct from team-owned records. Guild ADRs that recommend an organisation-wide direction still flow through the [[architecture-advisory-forum]] and the relevant decision logs; the guild does not bypass the standard decision path.

## Resources

The guild is provisioned with a small set of shared resources:

* **Slack channel** - a dedicated channel as the always-on front door. Membership is open. The channel is where questions, RFCs, study drafts, and event announcements live.
* **GitHub repositories** - guild-owned source code repositories for design-system contributions, reference implementations, shared SDKs, working-group experiments, and the experimentation-first artifacts the guild produces. Each repository has a named maintainer from the core organising group.
* **AWS account (or dedicated space)** - the guild has a dedicated AWS account, or a clearly carved-out space within an existing account, for shared experiments, reference deployments, beta-distribution infrastructure, and CI trials. Cost ownership and access boundaries are explicit; the account is not a back door to production environments.

These resources exist to **support shared work**, not to host production systems owned by individual teams. If something built by the guild graduates into a production system, ownership transfers to a team and the guild artefact becomes a reference.

App-store credentials, signing keys, and developer-program memberships are **not** owned by the guild. They remain with the teams or the platform group responsible for each brand's apps. The guild can recommend practices around them; it does not hold them.

## Relationship with Web Frontend

The Mobile Guild is **mobile-focused**. A separate [[frontend-guild]] owns the equivalent space for web frontend.

The split is deliberate:

* The technology stacks, release cycles, and tooling diverge enough that one guild for both would dilute both communities.
* Mobile has its own deep questions (store releases, native UI, offline, device APIs, signing) that are out of scope for a web consortium.
* A mobile-only frame keeps the platform-specific, store-delivery, and native-vs-cross-platform conversations sharp.

The guilds coordinate, not compete:

* Topics that genuinely span both (design tokens, brand consistency, shared API contracts, identity flows, analytics events) are jointly worked rather than absorbed by one guild.
* Both guilds share the same shape (purpose / mandate / composition / studies / resources) so cross-guild participation is low-friction.
* The architecture group acts as the coordination point when scope is genuinely contested.

If the boundary turns out to be wrong - for example, a cross-platform stack becomes the dominant frontend channel and no longer fits either guild cleanly - the foundations for both guilds should be revisited together.

## What This Means for Teams

For mobile engineers:

* The guild is the place to bring mobile questions that are bigger than one team. Use the Slack channel as the front door.
* Lessons learned, incidents, crashes, store-review surprises, and successful patterns are welcome at the bi-weekly session - the more concrete, the more useful for everyone else.
* Adopting a recommended standard or reference implementation is a default, not a mandate. If a team needs to deviate, the guild is the right place to surface why; deviations often reveal a gap in the standard itself.

For team leads:

* Encourage at least one delegate per mobile-heavy team to attend bi-weekly sessions, ideally with iOS and Android both represented over time.
* Bring upcoming non-trivial mobile decisions to the guild early enough for advice to change the outcome - much like the [[architecture-advisory-forum]] for cross-cutting decisions.
* Surface incidents and postmortems with mobile lessons so the rest of the community learns from them.

For the architecture group and Chief Architect:

* Treat the guild as the standing practitioner voice on mobile topics.
* Route relevant mobile recommendations and decisions through the guild for feedback before finalising.
* Resist the temptation to convert the guild into a steering committee; its value is in practice and in shared artefacts, not in approvals.

For new joiners:

* The guild is one of the fastest places to absorb how Organization does mobile work. Joining the Slack channel, watching a recorded session, and attending one bi-weekly is a low-cost, high-signal onboarding step.

## Anti-Patterns

The guild can quietly drift into something less useful. Watch for these patterns:

* **The guild becomes an approval board.** Teams stop deciding on mobile topics until the guild "agrees". Decision ownership has leaked from the teams to the guild.
* **Closed membership.** The bi-weekly becomes a small invite-only circle. The community narrows. The breadth that justifies the guild disappears.
* **Single-platform monoculture.** The guild only discusses iOS or only Android. Recommendations stop being credible for the rest of the organisation.
* **Cross-platform religion.** The guild becomes an advocacy group for one particular cross-platform stack rather than honestly weighing native and cross-platform tradeoffs.
* **Scope creep into web.** The guild starts running web sessions because the Frontend Guild is quieter, hollowing both communities instead of fixing the real issue in web.
* **Standards without owners.** A recommended standard exists in a repository, but no one is named to maintain it. It rots and teams stop trusting it.
* **External visibility without internal substance.** Conference talks proliferate; internal sharing and reference-implementation work do not. The guild becomes a personal-brand vehicle.
* **Meeting theatre.** Bi-weekly sessions run on time and produce no learning, decisions, or follow-ups. Attendance drops; cadence collapses.
* **Shadow architecture body.** The guild begins to make decisions that should flow through the [[architecture-advisory-forum]] or the relevant decision log, bypassing the standard advice and record flow.
* **Production by accident.** Something built in the guild's AWS account becomes load-bearing for a real product without an explicit ownership transfer.
* **Recordings nobody watches, notes nobody reads.** Transparency becomes ceremony. The artefacts exist but are not curated, indexed, or signposted.

Most failure modes share one shape: the guild slowly becomes either a closed club, a parallel governance body, or a scope-creeping super-guild. Resisting all three drifts is the lead's most important job.

## Success Indicators

The Mobile Guild is succeeding when:

* Mobile engineers across brands and across both major platforms say they feel part of one community.
* Recommended standards and reference implementations are maintained, named, and trusted; deviations are visible and discussed.
* Bi-weekly sessions are attended by a wide range of teams, recordings and notes are actually consumed, and contributions come from beyond the same few voices.
* iOS and Android perspectives are both regularly visible in sessions and artefacts.
* The guild's feedback is visibly reflected in architecture recommendations and decisions.
* New joiners onboard faster on mobile topics because the guild is a known front door.
* External talks and posts are coordinated, on-message, and connected to internal work.
* Ad-hoc working groups form, deliver, and dissolve cleanly when their topic is exhausted.
* Coordination with the [[frontend-guild]] and other architecture guilds is visible on cross-cutting topics.

The guild is **not** succeeding when:

* The same small group dominates every session.
* Only one platform is regularly represented.
* Standards are stale, unowned, or quietly ignored by most teams.
* Teams route mobile decisions through the guild as if it were an approval gate.
* The guild becomes a venue for status reporting rather than practice.
* External visibility outpaces internal learning.
* Web topics keep showing up because the Frontend Guild is not working - a sign to fix the Frontend Guild, not absorb its scope.

## Scope and Revisiting

This decision applies to **Organization's mobile practice across teams and brands**. It establishes a single Mobile Guild at the engineering-wide level. It does not preclude:

* Team-level mobile rituals.
* Platform-specific working groups inside the guild's umbrella (iOS-only or Android-only, time-bounded).
* Brand-specific design-system extensions, coordinated through the guild.
* Other guilds for other competencies (web, backend, data, platform), which should follow the same shape but live in their own foundations.

Out of scope:

* Web frontend (handled by the [[frontend-guild]]).
* Ownership of team-owned mobile apps.
* Ownership of app-store credentials, signing keys, or developer-program memberships.
* Approval of team-level architecture decisions.
* Replacement of the [[architecture-advisory-forum]] for cross-team architecture advice.
* Personnel decisions and performance management of guild members.

This decision should be revisited if:

* Attendance breadth or contribution rate degrades over a sustained period.
* Only one platform regularly shows up; the iOS/Android balance has broken in a structural way.
* The guild starts acting as an approval gate, or teams start routing decisions to it as if it were one.
* Recommended standards fall out of date with no clear path to refresh them.
* The web/mobile boundary becomes a recurring source of confusion - revisit the Frontend and Mobile Guild foundations together.
* The organisation grows or splits in ways that make a single mobile guild the wrong shape (for example, a brand-specific guild becomes a better fit).
* Other guilds form and the inter-guild coordination model needs to be made explicit.

A regular review of the guild itself - perhaps once a quarter alongside the [[architecture-advisory-forum]] retrospective - is healthy. The guild should be willing to advise on changes to its own format.
