AI is not an accelerator — a pedal that makes everything uniformly faster. It is an **amplifier**: a multiplier that scales whatever you already have. The arithmetic is `output = gain × input`, and multiplication preserves sign. Point AI at strength — a skilled person, a clean codebase, a clear goal, strong standards — and it compounds the strength. Point it at a mess — a brittle process, a confused goal, no review — and it compounds the mess, faster and more expensively. The leverage is never in the AI. It is in the input.

**What changes**

- **Stop treating AI adoption as a purchasing decision.** A bigger multiplier (better model, faster tool) on a weak input just reaches the wrong place sooner. The work is improving the input — skills, process, standards, direction — not chasing the multiplier.
- **Measure the sign before you scale.** AI's effect on speed runs both ways: large field studies show juniors gaining 27–39% while experienced developers on familiar code were measured *19% slower* — and believed they were 20% *faster*. Felt speedup is not real speedup. Pilot and measure before rolling out.
- **Budget for ownership, not generation.** Generating code is the cheap part AI makes cheaper; reviewing, testing, debugging, and maintaining far more code is the expensive part it can make costlier. Staff the downstream end.
- **Amplify the bottleneck, not the part that's already fast.** A system runs at the speed of its slowest stage. AI multiplies code generation, so the strain moves to review, integration, and fragile legacy — and an unbalanced system gets *worse*, not faster. This is the Theory of Constraints: improving a non-bottleneck just piles up work in front of the real constraint.
- **Don't mistake individual gains for team gains.** Developers reliably feel faster; team throughput often doesn't move (DORA's 2024 data even showed a negative system-level effect). The individual gain is real and then absorbed at the bottleneck and lost to coordination friction. Measure delivery to users, not keystrokes per developer.
- **Put standards upstream of the AI.** Evals, tests, and review are not a quality bonus you can skip — they decide whether the multiplier points up or down. Without them, you scale slop at full speed.
- **Expect AI to amplify your operating model.** The 2025 DORA study found AI magnifies the strengths of high-performing organizations and the dysfunction of struggling ones. The differentiator is never whether you have AI; it is the foundation it lands on.

**What it costs**

- Honesty about your own inputs. The model forces an uncomfortable question — *amplify what?* — and the answer may be "fix our foundations first."
- Patience against the speed narrative. Slower, measured rollout beats a fast rollout that amplifies a negative across the whole organization.

**What we are not doing**

- Not arguing AI is overrated or not worth it — the gains are real where the input is strong. This is a model correction, not a verdict.
- Not claiming AI is *only* a multiplier — it also adds genuinely new capability, and its "gain" is jagged, not one fixed number.
- Not offering precise cost figures — the maintenance and debt numbers are directional and attributed, not settled constants.
