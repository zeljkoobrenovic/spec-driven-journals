AI is automating the artifacts that once defined technology leadership — diagrams, decision memos, roadmap briefs, risk analyses. The leaders who stay valuable shift from artifact-makers to decision-makers, and the skills that carry that shift are **agency, judgment, and persuasion** (Jakob Nielsen's transition-period frame, applied to technology leadership).

The reason these three survive automation is structural: they sit where information meets accountability. AI can surface opportunities, compare options, and draft narratives — it cannot take responsibility for consequences, decide which trade-offs are legitimate for *this* organization, or make a decision feel legitimate to the people who must live with it.

**What changes**

- **Agency**: the era of waiting is over. Leaders start bounded experiments before there is a mandate, and shape AI adoption instead of having it happen *to* the organization.
- **Judgment**: AI produces ten plausible plans; the leader's value is eliminating the ones that won't fly given team capacity, governance, politics, and history. Polished output plus hype creates false confidence without it.
- **Persuasion**: being right is not enough. Technical direction becomes a decision only when it is legible to finance, product, and leadership — "launches features two weeks faster," not "Kubernetes is more scalable."
- The stakes apply across roles — principal/staff engineers, EMs, directors, VPs, CTOs, architects — different titles, same shift.

**What it costs**

- Deliberate practice now, before the playbook settles: a one-week loop (ask AI for three options, reject one in writing, stress-test risks, rewrite for a non-technical stakeholder, get peer feedback).
- Retiring low-value habits and some technical prestige in favor of future-relevant judgment.
- Accepting that how these skills are practiced will keep changing as AI capability evolves — the capabilities are durable, the practice is not.

**What we are not doing**

- Not a tool catalog, not a 2030 capability prediction, not an AI governance policy.
- Not claiming legacy skills are worthless overnight — expertise loses value when the work around it changes, which is a different claim.
- The scenarios are illustrative composites, not sourced case studies.
