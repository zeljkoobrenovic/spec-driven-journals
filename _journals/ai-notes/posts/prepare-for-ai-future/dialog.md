## The uncomfortable question

**Ben:** Ana, every architect I know has the same quiet worry: AI now drafts the diagram, the decision memo, the migration plan — in seconds. If the artifacts were the job, what's left?

**Ana:** That worry is the right starting point, and the post's answer is blunt: the artifacts were never the job. They were the *medium*. The job was always deciding what should happen next and getting people to move. AI just makes that distinction impossible to ignore — when production gets cheap, the value concentrates in evaluation, direction, and influence.

**Ben:** And the frame for that comes from Jakob Nielsen, right? Three skills?

**Ana:** Agency, judgment, and persuasion. He wrote it for UX careers in the AI transition; the post applies it to technology leadership — principal and staff engineers, EMs, directors, VPs, CTOs, architects. Different titles, same shift: less value in producing artifacts by hand, more value in deciding what happens next.

**Ben:** Obvious follow-up: if AI is so capable, why can't it do those three too?

**Ana:** Because all three sit where information meets accountability. A model can generate options, arguments, summaries, risks. It cannot be *responsible* for consequences. It can't repair trust after a bad call, doesn't know the informal history of your team, and can't decide which trade-offs are legitimate for your organization. AI expands the surface area of thinking; leaders still own direction, accountability, and consent.

## Agency: stop waiting

**Ben:** Take them one at a time. Agency — isn't that just "be proactive," the oldest career advice in the book?

**Ana:** The advice is old; the cost of ignoring it changed. There was a time a senior leader could wait for requirements, produce the reference model, review, escalate, done. AI doesn't wait, and neither do product teams. The post's scenario: a principal engineer at a logistics company sees latency and duplication across microservices. No formal initiative exists. She uses AI to trace dependencies, builds a small event-driven prototype with observability built in, and *then* pulls in the platform team. She doesn't ask if it's her job — she makes it her job.

**Ben:** And without that?

**Ana:** AI adoption becomes something that happens *to* the organization. Teams experiment in private, duplicate effort, argue about AI in the abstract, and the most risk-averse stakeholder sets the pace by default — because nobody has shown a responsible path forward.

## Judgment: ten options, one context

**Ben:** Judgment. The AI gives you ten plausible plans. So what — pick the highest-scored one?

**Ana:** That's exactly the trap. Plausibility is cheap now. The post's scenario: a VP asks AI for three rollout plans for AI-assisted development — centralize in a platform team, embed champions, or open access with light guardrails. On paper all three work. But she knows two product groups are under delivery pressure, security wants auditable usage, and managers haven't yet learned what good AI-assisted work looks like. She stages it: small enablement team, clear policy, visible pilots, manager coaching first. The model didn't know any of that context. It can't.

**Ben:** So the failure mode isn't AI suggesting something bad.

**Ana:** It's a leader accepting a plausible plan — or the fashionable one — without knowing whether the organization can execute it. Polished output plus hype equals false confidence. The judgment question is always: which option fits *these* teams, *this* risk tolerance, *this* history? And: what does the loudest metric hide — migration risk, learning cost, reversibility?

## Persuasion: right isn't enough

**Ben:** Persuasion always feels like the soft one. Why does it rank with the other two?

**Ana:** Because correct proposals die as private convictions all the time. The scenario: a staff engineer proposes a modular platform migration — containers, service meshes. Engineering nods; leadership shrugs. So he reframes: this reduces release friction, protects premium-customer SLAs, simplifies compliance reporting. Same proposal, suddenly legible to people who don't speak mesh. That's the skill — making the right decision evaluable and compelling for the people who must live with it.

**Ben:** "This lets us launch features two weeks faster" instead of "Kubernetes is more scalable."

**Ana:** That literal translation is in the post. And the manager version: don't say "use AI more" — connect it to a concrete workflow, name the guardrails, show how AI-assisted work gets reviewed. Influence happens in hallway chats and Slack threads, not just the big presentation.

## What to actually do this week

**Ben:** This all risks staying inspirational. Give me the Monday-morning version.

**Ana:** The post ends with a one-week practice loop, six steps. Pick one real decision you already owe someone. Ask AI for three *materially different* options, not one answer. Reject at least one and write down why it would fail in your context — that's judgment practice. Ask AI for risks, compare them with what your organization actually cares about. Rewrite the recommendation for a non-technical stakeholder — persuasion practice. Then share it with a peer and ask where your reasoning was weak.

**Ben:** And the longer arc? Nielsen has that mammoth-hunter line.

**Ana:** Around 2030, old skills may be the equivalent of being an experienced mammoth hunter. The point isn't that skills vanish overnight — it's that expertise loses value when the work around it changes. But the durable part is real too: agency, judgment, and persuasion persist while their *practice* keeps changing. Today's agency is starting a careful pilot; tomorrow's may be orchestrating agents across product, security, and operations. Today's judgment reviews generated plans; tomorrow's decides how much autonomy a system gets.

## What this isn't

**Ben:** Scope check before we close. What should nobody take from this?

**Ana:** It's not a tool catalog and not a prediction of exact 2030 capabilities. It's not an AI governance policy. It's explicitly *not* saying legacy skills are worthless — mastering systems that run the business still matters; clinging to outdated governance models is what doesn't. And the scenarios are illustrative composites, not case studies from named companies.

**Ben:** One line for the leader who reads nothing else?

**Ana:** Gregor Hohpe's principle, which the post closes on: you don't need to be the smartest person in the room — your job is making everyone else smarter, including the AI systems now in the room. Your value is no longer in drawing boxes. It's in drawing people together.

**Ben:** From mammoth hunter to people-gatherer. There are worse career arcs. *(laughs)*

**Ana:** And the leaders who start practicing now get to invent the playbook instead of waiting for it.
