# Spec: AI Will Do the Heavy Lifting. You Still Need to Lead

## Intent

Help technology leaders prepare for AI-shaped work by shifting attention from artifact production to agency, judgment, and persuasion. The post should create urgency without hype: AI can accelerate routine technical work, but leaders remain valuable when they initiate, evaluate, contextualize, and align people around decisions.

## Audience

- Principal engineers, staff engineers, and architects who increasingly guide technical direction through influence rather than formal authority.
- Engineering managers and engineering directors who need to align AI-shaped technical work with delivery, teams, and business outcomes.
- VPs of Engineering, CTOs, and other senior technology leaders deciding which skills to practice now rather than waiting for an AI operating model to become obvious.

Readers should leave with a practical self-assessment frame and a short list of habits to start practicing immediately.

## Success criteria

- [x] Reader understands agency, judgment, and persuasion as the three core skills the post emphasizes.
- [x] Reader can distinguish AI-generated outputs from human technical leadership accountability.
- [x] Reader understands why agency, judgment, and persuasion can be supported by AI but cannot simply be delegated to AI.
- [x] Reader sees what can go wrong when leaders lack agency, judgment, or persuasion, including hype-following, and what better leadership looks like in contrast.
- [x] Reader recognizes the article as relevant beyond architecture titles, including senior IC, management, and executive engineering roles.
- [x] Reader sees concrete role-specific stakes, a judgment comparison table, and a short practice loop they can try immediately.
- [x] Reader gets practical actions without being told that every legacy skill is worthless.
- [x] Reader understands that agency, judgment, and persuasion remain durable, but how leaders practice them will keep changing as AI capabilities evolve.
- [x] Source references to Jakob Nielsen and Gregor Hohpe are clear enough to distinguish quotation, paraphrase, and adaptation.
- [x] Illustrative scenarios are not presented as sourced case studies.
- [x] The post works as a standalone AI Notes article, not only as a book chapter.

## Non-goals

- A catalog of AI architecture tools.
- A prediction piece about exact AI capabilities in 2030.
- A policy for AI governance, security, or model selection.
- A claim that all legacy architecture skills should be abandoned immediately.
- A proof that the illustrative scenarios happened in named organizations.
