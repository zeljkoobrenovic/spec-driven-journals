When a generative model is part of the product itself — not just a tool developers use — development stops resembling stable long-term planning and starts resembling fashion: short seasons, changing materials, limited predictability, and taste-driven, evaluation-heavy judgment. Model-powered products may need frequent reinterpretation around new materials.

The distinction that matters: **AI-assisted development** (AI helps developers ship faster) is a productivity shift. **AI-powered product development** (customer value depends directly on the model) is an operating-model shift. A new model release isn't a better tool for the team — it's a new material for the product, and it can change workflows, interfaces, quality bars, unit economics, and the competitive position at once.

**What changes**

- Roadmaps become **seasonal portfolios**: stable commitments separated from seasonal bets and expiring model assumptions.
- Product briefs become **value hypotheses with evaluation contracts**: the material change, the customer outcome, the promise to preserve, the evaluation suite, and the stop condition — decided before rebuilding.
- Discovery adds **model-material scouting** alongside customer discovery: capability probes, cost/latency checks, failure-mode exploration, competitor patterns.
- Model upgrades become **migrations, not drop-ins**: a model that is better overall can be worse for your golden scenarios, expert controls, safety boundaries, latency targets, or margins.
- The **evaluation suite becomes a strategic asset** — golden scenarios, failure galleries, side-by-side comparisons, business dashboards — co-owned by product, design, and engineering.

**What it costs**

- Continuous material scouting and short-cycle re-planning instead of multi-quarter certainty.
- Evaluation artifacts and learning archives maintained as versioned, first-class product assets.
- Accepting limited predictability: customer taste, timing, culture, and competitor moves cannot be derived from model benchmarks.

**What we are not doing**

- Not fad-driven development: no integrating every release, no rebuilding for novelty, no shipping demos as product.
- Not replacing outcome-focused product management, durable architecture, testing, or customer trust — this extends them for unstable model materials.
- Not claiming every software team should rebuild continuously; the stable core (promise, quality bar, trust boundary, brand) is what makes fast rebuilding safe.
