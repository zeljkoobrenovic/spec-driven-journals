Generative AI gives us raw power the way a reactor core gives off heat — dense, impressive, and on its own **not yet useful**. The U.S. nuclear regulator describes a reactor core plainly as "the heat source for the power plant, just like the boiler is for a coal plant." Its output only becomes valuable once it is made "predictable and controllable." The same is becoming true of AI: the model is the core; **the product, and most of the work, is the plant built around it.**

**The fact that anchors the analogy**

* Most of a nuclear plant — by cost, components, and engineering — is **not** the reactor. The World Nuclear Association puts the "nuclear island" at about 28% of plant cost; turbine, balance of plant, and civil works together exceed it. A 1980 DOE breakdown (illustrative, and a share of *direct* cost — a different base) put the reactor itself at just 15–20%, co-equal with the turbine and structures, with about a third of total cost being indirect engineering and management.
* The reactor matters enormously — and yet produces nothing sellable until the plant around it controls, channels, and contains it.

**The three jobs of the plant**

* **Control** the rate — control rods, moderator, coolant. In AI: prompts, tool permissions, retrieval, orchestration. The throttle.
* **Channel** raw output into useful work — coolant loops to a turbine. In AI: evaluations, schemas, structured outputs, workflows that turn raw generation into a checkable result.
* **Contain** — three physical barriers, each assuming the one inside it can fail. In AI: sandboxes, output filters, human review, rate limits, rollback.

**Defense in depth**

* Nuclear safety never relies on one safeguard. The IAEA stacks five independent layers and mandates at least two diverse, independent shutdown systems. The AI lesson is identical: **assume each guardrail fails, and design the next to still hold** — not "add more filters."

**It widens — carefully**

* For an **organization**, value comes from the operating model around the model: accountability, review, who decides. For **society**, capability is arriving faster than the institutions that channel it. This is a claim about *structure*, not danger.

**Where it breaks**

* AI is **not** physically dangerous like fission — it cannot melt down or explode; its risks are wrong outputs, misuse, and over-trust. A model's behavior is not fixed physics; it shifts between versions. And unlike nuclear, AI's "plant" is being built in public without a settled rulebook. A bare model is still useful in a way a bare reactor core is not.

**What to do Monday**

* Budget for the plant, not the core (most work is evals, guardrails, orchestration, observability — not prompting). Build the throttle before scaling. Layer safeguards. Measure whether raw output becomes a *correct, checkable* result. Name who is accountable when an agent acts.

Generative AI handed us a reactor core. The work ahead is the plant — and that is not the boring part of the AI revolution. **It is the revolution.**
