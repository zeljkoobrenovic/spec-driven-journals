> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization will use communities of practice and chapters to maintain shared engineering craft across stream-aligned teams. Communities advise, teach, curate standards, review practices, and maintain reusable knowledge. They do not replace product teams, create hidden reporting lines, or become delivery queues.

## Decision

Adopt **communities of practice and chapters** as the cross-team mechanism for shared craft.

The default model:

* stream-aligned teams remain the primary unit of delivery and ownership;
* communities connect people with similar disciplines or concerns across teams;
* communities produce durable artifacts: ADRs, principles, standards, playbooks, templates, examples, learning material, and recommendations;
* communities provide advice through visible rituals and records;
* chapter-like structures may support career development, mentoring, and consistency for a discipline where the organisation needs that formality.

Existing foundation records such as [[backend-guild]], [[frontend-guild]], [[mobile-guild]], and [[genai-guild]] are examples of this direction.

## How to Read This Decision

| What it says | What it does **not** say |
| --- | --- |
| Shared craft needs a cross-team home. | Product teams stop owning delivery decisions. |
| Communities can define standards and advice. | Communities can silently override accountable teams. |
| Chapters may support careers and skills. | Chapters are automatically separate reporting lines. |
| Communities should produce durable artifacts. | Meetings are enough. |
| Participation should create learning. | Every engineer must attend every guild. |

Communities are useful when they create leverage. They are wasteful when they become calendar rituals without outcomes.

## How to Use This Decision

Use this decision to:

* explain why durable product teams still need cross-team craft mechanisms;
* distinguish communities of practice from reporting lines, delivery teams, and approval boards;
* recognise the outputs that make a community useful;
* decide when a community recommendation should become a standard, ADR, or platform capability;
* identify when a guild or chapter has become ceremony without leverage.

The practical lesson is that shared craft needs a home, but that home must improve product teams rather than compete with them.

## One-Minute Mental Model

A community of practice is a **learning and standard-setting amplifier**.

It connects people who solve similar problems in different teams. It finds repeated pain, turns local learning into shared material, helps people grow in their craft, and gives advice in public.

It should not become a second org chart. A healthy community makes teams better at owning their work. An unhealthy one pulls ownership away from teams or produces meetings without durable artifacts.

## Rationale

Stream-aligned teams improve ownership, but they can also fragment craft. Without a cross-team mechanism, backend patterns diverge, frontend practices drift, mobile quality varies, AI-assisted engineering evolves inconsistently, and new engineers learn only the habits of their immediate team.

Communities solve this without recentralising delivery. They give the organisation a place to:

* compare patterns across teams;
* identify repeated pain;
* turn good local practice into shared standards;
* create learning paths and mentoring;
* review external technology signals;
* steward technical principles and defaults;
* support career development for specialists embedded in product teams.

The important boundary is that communities advise and enable. They do not become the place where product work is approved or reassigned.

## Community Outputs

Healthy communities produce visible outputs:

| Output | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Standards | Testing baseline, frontend accessibility checklist, API versioning guidance. |
| Golden paths | Starter templates, reference pipelines, approved library patterns. |
| Learning material | Onboarding path, internal workshops, recorded examples. |
| Decision advice | Proposed ADRs, advice notes, revisits of old decisions. |
| Practice reviews | Periodic review of health in a discipline, with follow-up actions. |
| Talent support | Interview rubrics, career expectations, mentoring circles. |
| External sensing | Technology radar input, vendor evaluation, deprecation warnings. |

If a community has no outputs beyond meeting notes, its purpose should be revisited.

## Community Authority

Communities may:

* recommend standards;
* maintain playbooks and templates;
* advise on ADRs;
* identify gaps in team capability;
* raise risks to engineering leadership;
* own reusable capabilities only when a named team or group accepts lifecycle responsibility.

Communities may not:

* own product delivery for stream-aligned teams;
* act as hidden approval boards;
* impose standards without an adoption path;
* publish shared capabilities with no owner;
* become a substitute for management, architecture, or platform ownership.

When a community recommendation should become a real constraint, it must be turned into an ADR, principle, platform guardrail, or leadership decision with ownership.

## Worked Example

The frontend community notices that several teams solve accessibility inconsistently.

Weak community response:

* A guild meeting discusses accessibility.
* Notes are posted in chat.
* Each team interprets the advice differently.
* The same problem returns next quarter.

Stronger community response:

| Step | Output |
| --- | --- |
| Collect examples | Short comparison of current accessibility gaps across teams. |
| Draft guidance | A frontend accessibility checklist and examples. |
| Create support path | Office-hours slot for teams applying the checklist. |
| Encode defaults | Starter components and lint checks where possible. |
| Review adoption | Follow up after one quarter with evidence and updates. |

The community does not take over frontend delivery. It improves the shared practice and lowers the effort for every team.

## Operating Rhythm

Each community should define a lightweight operating model:

| Element | Minimum expectation |
| --- | --- |
| Purpose | Why the community exists and which discipline or concern it stewards. |
| Lead or stewards | Named people who facilitate, curate, and follow up. |
| Cadence | Regular enough to be useful, light enough to avoid ceremony. |
| Backlog | Visible list of topics, standards, learning needs, and decisions. |
| Outputs | Where durable artifacts live. |
| Feedback loop | How teams request advice and report friction. |
| Review | Periodic check whether the community still creates value. |

The community should have enough structure to be reliable, but not enough process to become a parallel organisation.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Guild as talking shop.** Interesting conversations happen, but nothing changes in teams or artifacts.
* **Hidden approval board.** Teams learn they need a guild blessing even though no decision says so.
* **Standard without owner.** The community publishes a rule but nobody maintains the supporting capability.
* **Attendance tax.** People attend out of obligation even when the agenda has no relevance.
* **Craft silo.** Members identify more with the chapter than with the product outcomes their team owns.
* **One-person community.** A passionate individual carries everything with no succession or ownership.

## Discussion Prompts

Use these prompts when starting or reviewing a community:

* What shared craft problem does this community solve?
* Which teams benefit from the community's work?
* What durable outputs did the community produce this quarter?
* Which recommendations need owners, adoption paths, or automation?
* Is the community helping teams own work better, or pulling decisions away from them?
* What should the community stop doing because it creates no leverage?

## Scope and Revisiting

This ADR applies to engineering communities, chapters, guilds, and similar cross-team practice groups.

Revisit if communities become approval gates, if shared craft fragments despite them, or if career development needs require a more formal chapter model.
