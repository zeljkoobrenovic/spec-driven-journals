> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization engineering will use a lightweight goal-setting and planning cadence that connects strategy to team work. Goals express outcomes and constraints, not task lists. Teams use a small number of objectives and measurable results, review progress regularly, and separate goal learning from individual performance scoring.

## Decision

Adopt a **goal-setting and planning cadence** for engineering.

The cadence uses:

* annual or multi-quarter strategic direction;
* quarterly team or domain goals;
* monthly progress reviews;
* weekly or biweekly planning inside teams;
* retrospectives or goal reviews at the end of each cycle;
* explicit connection to product outcomes, engineering health, platform investment, and capability development.

The default vocabulary may use OKRs where useful: objectives describe the meaningful outcome; key results describe measurable evidence that the outcome changed. Teams may use another goal format if it preserves the same discipline.

## How to Read This Decision

| What it says | What it does **not** say |
| --- | --- |
| Goals should create focus and alignment. | Every activity must become an OKR. |
| Goals should express outcomes. | Teams never track delivery milestones. |
| Progress should be reviewed regularly. | Goals should create reporting theatre. |
| Team goals should guide priority decisions. | Individual goals become performance scores by default. |
| Goals should adapt when evidence changes. | Goals can be rewritten casually to avoid accountability. |

The goal system is useful only when it helps teams say yes and no.

## How to Use This Decision

Use this decision to:

* distinguish outcomes, outputs, milestones, metrics, and activities;
* write a small set of goals that creates focus rather than a wishlist;
* connect engineering health work to product and strategy goals;
* run goal reviews as learning conversations;
* avoid turning OKRs into individual performance targets or task lists.

The practical lesson is that goal setting is a decision system. It decides what matters enough to protect from everything else.

## One-Minute Mental Model

Goals are **focus contracts**.

A good goal says: this outcome matters enough that we will align work, tradeoffs, and attention around it for a defined period. A weak goal says: here are many things we hope to do.

Key results are evidence, not activity. "Migrate 20 services" may be a milestone. "Reduce deployment lead time for the domain from five days to one day" is a result. Both may matter, but confusing them makes the organisation look busy without proving progress.

## Rationale

Engineering organisations fail goal setting in two opposite ways:

* goals are so vague that teams cannot use them to make tradeoffs;
* goals are so detailed that they become a project plan and lose strategic value.

The right middle ground gives teams direction, measures, and constraints while leaving room for discovery and engineering judgement.

Goal setting also connects strategy to capacity. If every team has ten goals, the organisation has not chosen. If product goals ignore reliability, platform, technical debt, and capability development, the organisation will create short-term delivery at the cost of long-term flow. If engineering goals are detached from product and business outcomes, technical work becomes hard to explain and defend.

## Goal Hierarchy

| Level | Purpose | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Company / business | Strategic direction and market focus. | Improve trust and conversion in the core marketplace. |
| Domain / product area | Outcomes and constraints for a value stream. | Reduce listing-search abandonment while preserving relevance and latency. |
| Team | Local outcomes the team can influence. | Improve saved-search activation and reduce alert false positives. |
| Engineering health | Flow, reliability, quality, platform, and capability outcomes. | Cut change lead time for checkout services by 50 percent. |
| Individual development | Growth and capability, not performance scoring by OKR. | Build incident leadership skill by leading two post-incident reviews. |

Individual development goals may exist, but they should not turn company OKRs into personal scorecards.

## Outcome, Output, Activity, and Metric

Teams often use these words loosely. Keep the distinctions explicit:

| Term | Meaning | Example |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Outcome | A meaningful change in user, business, team, or system state. | Serious seekers return to relevant listings faster. |
| Output | A thing delivered. | Saved-search notification feature shipped. |
| Activity | Work performed. | Run five discovery interviews, migrate templates, hold workshops. |
| Metric | A signal used to observe change. | Activation rate, false-positive reports, latency, unsubscribe rate. |
| Milestone | A planned point in delivery. | Beta released to 10 percent of users by week six. |

Outputs and activities can be necessary. They become weak goals when they are mistaken for outcomes.

## Goal Quality Checklist

| Question | Good sign |
| --- | --- |
| Is the objective meaningful? | A human can explain why it matters. |
| Is the result measurable? | The team can observe progress without inventing fake precision. |
| Is it limited? | The team has one to three real priorities, not a catalogue. |
| Is it influenceable? | The team can materially affect the result. |
| Does it include constraints? | Reliability, quality, compliance, and cost are not afterthoughts. |
| Does it create tradeoffs? | The goal helps say no to lower-priority work. |
| Is there a review cadence? | Progress is discussed before the quarter ends. |

If a goal does not change decisions, it is probably decoration.

## Goal Writing Template

Use this template for team goals:

| Element | Prompt |
| --- | --- |
| Objective | What meaningful outcome are we trying to create? |
| Why now | Why does this matter this cycle? |
| Key results | Which two to four signals would show progress? |
| Constraints | Which reliability, quality, compliance, cost, or people constraints matter? |
| Dependencies | Which teams, platforms, or decisions could block progress? |
| Tradeoffs | What will we stop, defer, or protect? |
| Review rhythm | When will we inspect evidence and adapt? |

The template is intentionally small. If a goal needs a long narrative to be understood, it probably needs sharper framing.

## Operating Rhythm

| Cadence | Activity |
| --- | --- |
| Annual / multi-quarter | Confirm strategic themes, investment areas, and major constraints. |
| Quarterly | Set team/domain goals, review capacity, identify dependencies, record tradeoffs. |
| Monthly | Review progress, blockers, metrics, and whether assumptions changed. |
| Weekly / biweekly | Plan work, adjust WIP, and connect current work to goals. |
| End of cycle | Score or summarise results, capture learning, and decide what changes next. |

Goal reviews should produce decisions: continue, stop, adapt, invest, or revisit assumptions.

## Goal Review Questions

During a monthly review, ask:

* What changed in the metric, and what do we believe caused it?
* Which assumption became weaker or stronger?
* Which dependency or constraint is now the main blocker?
* What work should stop because it no longer serves the goal?
* What should leadership know because the goal is at risk?
* What did we learn that should affect next quarter's planning?

The review is not a status ceremony. It is a decision point.

## Worked Example

Weak goal:

* "Deliver saved-search notifications."

This is an output. It may be useful, but it does not say why the work matters or how success will be recognised.

Stronger goal:

| Element | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Objective | Help serious seekers return to relevant listings faster. |
| Key result 1 | Increase saved-search activation among signed-in seekers from 18 percent to 28 percent. |
| Key result 2 | Reduce notification false-positive reports by 30 percent. |
| Key result 3 | Keep alert-delivery latency under the agreed SLO. |
| Constraints | No increase in unsubscribe rate; follow privacy and communication rules. |

The stronger goal supports product, engineering, quality, and operational tradeoffs.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Goal soup.** Every priority becomes an objective, so nothing is actually prioritised.
* **Task-list OKRs.** Key results are milestones or activities with no outcome evidence.
* **Individual OKR scoring.** Team goals become a personal performance game.
* **Quarterly theatre.** Goals are written, presented, and ignored until the next cycle.
* **Metric tunnel vision.** Teams optimise one number while damaging reliability, quality, or customer trust.
* **Strategy fog.** Teams are asked to set goals without clear company or domain direction.

## Practice Exercise

Take one current team goal and rewrite it:

1. Mark each phrase as outcome, output, activity, metric, or milestone.
2. If the goal is mostly output or activity, write the outcome it is meant to create.
3. Add one quality or reliability constraint.
4. Remove any key result the team cannot influence.
5. Name one tradeoff the goal forces.
6. Decide what evidence will be reviewed monthly.

## Discussion Prompts

Use these prompts in planning:

* Which outcome matters enough to protect this quarter?
* What are we explicitly not doing because this goal matters?
* Which result would prove progress, and which metric is only a proxy?
* Which reliability, quality, cost, or compliance constraints must be visible?
* Which dependencies could block this goal?
* What will we learn at the end even if the goal is not fully achieved?

## Scope and Revisiting

This ADR applies to engineering goals, domain goals involving engineering teams, and team planning. Company-wide strategy and finance planning remain broader leadership processes that this cadence should connect to.

Revisit if goals become too many, too output-driven, detached from strategy, misused for individual scoring, or ignored during execution.
