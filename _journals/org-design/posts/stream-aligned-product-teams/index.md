> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization will use durable stream-aligned product teams as the default unit of software engineering. A team owns a coherent stream of value from discovery through build, release, operation, learning, and retirement. Temporary project teams and specialist handoff queues may exist for specific transitions, but they are not the default shape of the engineering organisation.

## Decision

Adopt **durable stream-aligned product teams** as the primary organisational unit for modern software engineering at Organization.

A stream-aligned product team:

* owns a customer, business, internal-product, or platform value stream;
* contains enough skills to change and run its software without routine handoffs;
* has a stable mission, not only a temporary project assignment;
* participates in product discovery and product delivery;
* owns production outcomes for its services and capabilities;
* makes local technical decisions through the Advice Process and records durable decisions as markdown ADRs.

This decision sets the default. It does not mean every group is a stream-aligned team. Platform, enabling, and complicated-subsystem teams remain valid where the work genuinely calls for them. Those exceptions are covered in [[team-topologies-operating-model]].

## How to Read This Decision

| What it says | What it does **not** say |
| --- | --- |
| Teams should be durable around value streams. | Teams never change shape. |
| Teams own discovery, delivery, and operation inside their boundary. | Every team must contain every specialist full time. |
| Teams receive problems and outcomes, not only feature tickets. | Product strategy disappears from leadership. |
| Teams own their local decisions. | Teams ignore shared principles or platform standards. |
| Temporary project groups are exceptions. | Migration, incident, and transition work cannot be organised temporarily. |

The core shift is from staffing projects to funding and improving long-lived teams.

## How to Use This Decision

Use this decision to:

* explain why durable teams are different from project staffing;
* identify whether a team owns a real stream of value or only a technical fragment;
* name the minimum responsibilities that come with product-team ownership;
* spot when a temporary team is useful and when it has become organisational debt;
* connect team ownership to decision ownership, production ownership, and capability lifecycle.

The test is practical: a new engineer should be able to read the team's mission and know what the team owns, who it serves, which systems it runs, and what kinds of decisions it can make.

## One-Minute Mental Model

A stream-aligned product team is a **small, durable business-and-technology unit wrapped around a flow of value**.

It is not a pool of engineers waiting for tickets. It is not a temporary project group. It is not a component team that only owns one technical layer. It is the place where product context, technical context, operational history, and customer learning accumulate over time.

If the team cannot explain its users, outcomes, owned systems, support responsibilities, and decision rights, it is not yet a real stream-aligned team. It may be a workgroup, component team, or project team using product-team language.

## Rationale

Software delivery improves when the group that understands the problem also owns the system, the customer impact, and the production consequences. Fragmenting those responsibilities creates predictable failure modes:

* product throws requirements over a wall to engineering;
* engineering throws releases over a wall to operations;
* QA is asked to find quality after the design is complete;
* architecture appears as a late approval step;
* knowledge evaporates when a project ends;
* teams optimise for local task completion instead of product and operational outcomes.

Durable stream-aligned teams reduce those handoffs. They create a stable place for learning to accumulate: product context, domain language, architecture tradeoffs, operational history, customer feedback, and team working agreements.

This ADR also aligns org design with [[capabilities-follow-ownership]] and [[decisions-stay-with-teams]]. If a team is accountable for outcomes, it must also be close enough to the relevant decisions and capabilities to act.

## Team Mission

Every stream-aligned team should have a short team mission that answers:

| Question | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| What value stream do we own? | Prevents teams from becoming generic capacity pools. |
| Who are our users or customers? | Keeps the team connected to outcomes. |
| Which systems or capabilities do we own? | Makes operational ownership explicit. |
| What decisions can we make locally? | Reduces escalation and hidden approvals. |
| Which teams depend on us, and who do we depend on? | Makes collaboration and team APIs visible. |

The mission should be stable enough to survive quarterly planning, but not so permanent that it blocks learning or reorganisation when the business changes.

## What This Means for Teams

For product engineering teams:

* Keep product discovery and delivery in the same team.
* Own production readiness, observability, supportability, and retirement work for the systems in the team boundary.
* Maintain ADRs for meaningful team decisions.
* Use shared platforms and standards by default, and propose exceptions through visible advice.

For product and engineering leaders:

* Fund stable teams, not only projects.
* Give teams objectives, constraints, and context, not only implementation backlogs.
* Review whether team boundaries still match value streams.
* Avoid reorganising as a substitute for hard prioritisation.

For platform and enabling teams:

* Treat stream-aligned teams as primary customers.
* Make the easy path self-service where possible.
* Reduce cognitive load rather than centralising all expertise.

## Worked Example

Imagine a team called "Search Experience".

Weak version:

* The team receives tickets from product and frontend requests from other teams.
* Backend search infrastructure is owned elsewhere.
* Analytics are reviewed by product outside the team.
* Production incidents go to an operations queue.
* The team has no authority to change ranking, indexing, or release strategy.

This is not a stream-aligned team. It is a delivery slice inside a larger flow.

Stronger version:

* The team owns the customer search journey and the main search-experience metrics.
* Product, design, engineering, and quality work from the same problem statements.
* The team owns the services, UI surfaces, dashboards, alerts, and runbooks inside the boundary.
* Platform teams provide search infrastructure capabilities through clear interfaces.
* Architecture advice is sought for cross-team ranking, data, and performance decisions.

The stronger version has more responsibility, but also less handoff. It can learn faster because the people changing the product see the product and operational consequences.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Project staffing carousel.** People are assembled for a project, disbanded when it ships, and the operational consequences land somewhere else.
* **Feature factory team.** The team receives a stream of predefined features but has little access to customer, business, or technical strategy.
* **Component bunker.** A team owns a technical component so narrowly that delivering customer value requires many handoffs.
* **Pseudo-ownership.** A team is named as owner but lacks authority, product context, operational access, or time to improve the system.
* **Permanent temporary team.** A migration group exists for years without becoming a real value-stream or platform team.

## Discussion Prompts

Use these prompts in leadership reviews or team retrospectives:

* What stream of value does this team own in one sentence?
* Which customer, user, partner, or internal consumer would notice if the team stopped existing?
* Which systems or capabilities does the team operate in production?
* What decisions can the team make without escalation?
* Which handoffs repeatedly slow the team down?
* Is the team funded as a durable unit, or is it really a project group?

## Scope and Revisiting

This decision applies to engineering groups building, integrating, operating, or enabling software products and platforms.

Revisit it if teams repeatedly cannot own discovery, delivery, and operations inside their boundaries; if value streams change materially; or if a future operating model makes another team shape a better default.
