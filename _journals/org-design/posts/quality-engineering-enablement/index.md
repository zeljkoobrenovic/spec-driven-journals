> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization will treat quality as a team-owned engineering responsibility, supported by quality engineering enablement. QA is not a late release gate that receives finished work. Quality engineers help teams design risk-based test strategies, automate confidence, improve observability, run exploratory testing, and strengthen release discipline.

## Decision

Adopt **quality engineering as enablement**.

The default model:

* product teams own the quality of what they ship and run;
* quality engineers work with teams before and during implementation, not only after;
* test automation is part of engineering work, not a separate afterthought;
* exploratory testing, risk analysis, accessibility, performance, resilience, and production learning all contribute to release confidence;
* a central or chapter-like quality function may set standards, coach teams, and improve shared tooling, but it does not become a permanent release queue.

This decision changes the meaning of QA from "the people who approve releases" to "the capability that helps teams understand and manage product risk".

## How to Read This Decision

| What it says | What it does **not** say |
| --- | --- |
| Teams own quality. | Specialist quality skills are unnecessary. |
| QE helps design confidence early. | Manual exploratory testing has no value. |
| Automation should be close to the team. | Every test must be written by developers only. |
| Release gates should be lightweight and evidence-based. | Teams may release without discipline. |
| Central QE should enable, coach, and improve systems. | Central QE becomes a dumping ground for unfinished work. |

Quality is not a phase. It is a property of how the team works.

## How to Use This Decision

Use this decision to:

* explain why quality cannot be delegated to a late QA phase;
* distinguish quality assurance as gatekeeping from quality engineering as capability building;
* design a release confidence model using several signals, not one test stage;
* identify where specialist quality skill should be embedded, shared, or enabling;
* use defects and incidents as learning inputs for the whole team.

The main learning is that "who tests this?" is too narrow. The better question is "how does the team build justified confidence that this change is valuable, safe, usable, and operable?"

## One-Minute Mental Model

Quality engineering is **risk thinking plus feedback design**.

Some risks are best handled by unit tests. Some need contract tests, exploratory testing, observability, accessibility checks, performance testing, rollout controls, or better product discovery. A quality engineer helps the team choose the right feedback loops for the risks in front of it.

The role is strongest when it changes how the team works. If quality remains a person at the end of the line, the organisation has changed the title but not the system.

## Rationale

Late QA gates create poor incentives. Engineering optimises for handing work over; QA becomes the place where defects are found after context has cooled; product pressure turns testing into a negotiation; release confidence depends on heroics.

Modern product engineering needs faster feedback:

* requirements and edge cases are challenged during discovery;
* testability influences design;
* automated checks run near the change;
* observability shows whether production behaves as expected;
* exploratory testing is focused on meaningful risk rather than regression ritual;
* defects teach the team how to improve the system, not only how to patch a release.

The quality function remains important. It becomes more senior, more systemic, and more influential when it shifts from gatekeeping to capability building.

## Quality Engineering Responsibilities

Quality engineering contributes at several levels:

| Level | QE contribution |
| --- | --- |
| Product discovery | Risk framing, edge cases, acceptance criteria, accessibility and usability concerns. |
| Architecture and design | Testability, observability, isolation, data setup, resilience and rollback considerations. |
| Implementation | Automation strategy, pairing, contract tests, test data, flake reduction, CI signal quality. |
| Release | Evidence-based release confidence, exploratory sessions, risk sign-off when needed. |
| Production | Defect analysis, monitoring feedback, incident learning, quality trends. |
| Organisation | Standards, coaching, shared tooling, community practice, hiring and career expectations. |

The same person does not need to perform every responsibility. The organisation must ensure the responsibilities are covered.

## Team Operating Model

For most stream-aligned teams, use one of three patterns:

| Pattern | Use when | Risk |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Embedded QE | Product risk is high, domain is complex, or the team is still building quality capability. | QE becomes the only person allowed to care about quality. |
| Shared QE enablement | Several mature teams need coaching, strategy reviews, and targeted help. | Support becomes too thin or reactive. |
| QE chapter/community | The organisation needs standards, practices, and career growth for quality specialists. | Community advice is ignored because it has no path into team work. |

The chosen pattern should be explicit and reviewed. A team with no quality strategy should not claim it has "embedded quality" just because developers write some tests.

## Release Confidence Model

Each team should maintain a release confidence model:

| Signal | Example |
| --- | --- |
| Unit and component checks | Fast tests close to the changed code. |
| Contract and integration checks | Confidence at service boundaries without depending on brittle end-to-end suites. |
| End-to-end checks | Few, high-value journeys that prove critical paths. |
| Exploratory testing | Human investigation of new risk, not rote regression. |
| Observability | Dashboards, alerts, logs, traces, and product telemetry for post-release learning. |
| Rollback or mitigation | A credible path when release assumptions fail. |

The model should follow [[production-ready]] and [[observable-by-default]]. It should be reviewed after escaped defects and incidents.

## Worked Example

A team is changing checkout address validation.

Late-gate QA pattern:

* Developers implement the change.
* QA receives a ticket near the end.
* Manual regression checks focus on known happy paths.
* A production defect appears for an edge-case address.

Quality-engineering pattern:

| Moment | QE contribution |
| --- | --- |
| Discovery | Ask which address formats, countries, partners, and customer journeys are affected. |
| Design | Identify validation rules, test data needs, observability, rollback, and support impact. |
| Implementation | Pair on boundary tests, contract tests, and risky combinations. |
| Release | Run exploratory sessions around edge cases and define rollout monitoring. |
| After release | Review support contacts, validation failures, and alert data for missed assumptions. |

The second pattern does not mean more ceremony. It means the team thinks about risk while it can still design better feedback.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Throw it to QA.** Finished work is handed to QA after design and implementation decisions are already fixed.
* **Manual regression blanket.** Large manual scripts are used as a substitute for automated confidence and risk thinking.
* **Automation theatre.** Many tests exist, but they are slow, flaky, and not trusted.
* **Developer-only dogma.** Specialist testing, exploratory skill, and risk analysis are dismissed as old-fashioned.
* **Quality as permission.** QA is made responsible for release approval without authority over product pressure or engineering design.
* **No production feedback.** The team treats defects as testing failures but never improves observability, rollback, or system design.

## Discussion Prompts

Use these prompts when reviewing a team's quality model:

* What are the highest product, technical, and operational risks in this work?
* Which feedback loop catches each risk earliest and cheapest?
* Which tests are trusted, and which are ignored because they are slow or flaky?
* What do escaped defects teach us about discovery, design, implementation, tests, or observability?
* Does a quality specialist improve the team's capability, or become the owner of quality by default?
* What production signal tells us that quality is holding after release?

## Scope and Revisiting

This ADR applies to software product, platform, and integration teams. It does not eliminate compliance, security, or regulatory gates where they are required; it requires those gates to be explicit, risk-based, and integrated into the team workflow as early as possible.

Revisit if escaped defects rise, release confidence degrades, teams lack testing skill, or a central QA queue reappears as the default path to production.
