> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization will use Team Topologies as the shared operating vocabulary for engineering organisation design. Stream-aligned teams are the default. Platform, enabling, and complicated-subsystem teams are explicit exceptions used to reduce cognitive load and accelerate flow. Interaction modes - collaboration, X-as-a-service, and facilitating - must be named rather than left implicit.

## Decision

Adopt **Team Topologies** as the language for describing engineering team types and interactions.

Organization will use four team types:

| Team type | Purpose at Organization |
| --- | --- |
| Stream-aligned team | Owns a flow of customer, business, internal-product, or platform value. Default team type. |
| Platform team | Provides an internal product that accelerates stream-aligned teams and reduces their cognitive load. |
| Enabling team | Temporarily helps other teams acquire capability, improve practice, or adopt a new pattern. |
| Complicated-subsystem team | Owns a part of the system requiring deep specialist knowledge that would overload stream-aligned teams. |

Organization will use three interaction modes:

| Interaction mode | Use when |
| --- | --- |
| Collaboration | Two or more teams need to discover a new boundary, solve an uncertain problem, or coordinate a significant change. |
| X-as-a-service | One team consumes a stable capability from another through a clear interface or team API. |
| Facilitating | An enabling team helps another team learn, adopt, or improve without taking permanent ownership. |

## How to Read This Decision

| What it says | What it does **not** say |
| --- | --- |
| Team types and interaction modes should be explicit. | The org chart must be redrawn to match a book diagram. |
| Platform teams build internal products. | Every infrastructure or operations group is automatically a platform team. |
| Enabling teams transfer capability. | Enabling teams become permanent delivery proxies. |
| Complicated-subsystem teams are rare and justified by cognitive load. | Specialists should be isolated by default. |
| Interaction modes can change over time. | Team relationships are fixed forever. |

The vocabulary is useful only if it changes behaviour: fewer accidental dependencies, clearer ownership, and lower cognitive load.

## How to Use This Decision

Use this decision to:

* distinguish the four Team Topologies team types in Organization terms;
* identify the interaction mode a team relationship is currently using;
* explain why platform and enabling teams exist to reduce cognitive load, not to collect work;
* detect when a renamed silo is being presented as a platform team;
* use topology review as a way to improve flow across teams.

The practical skill is diagnosis: when delivery slows down, ask whether the blocker is a team boundary, an interaction mode, a platform gap, or excessive cognitive load.

## One-Minute Mental Model

Team Topologies is a **map of how work flows through teams**.

The team type describes why the team exists. The interaction mode describes how two teams work together right now. Both can change. A platform team may collaborate with early adopters while building a capability, then move to X-as-a-service when the interface stabilises. An enabling team may facilitate adoption for a quarter, then leave when the receiving team can operate independently.

The vocabulary prevents vague sentences like "platform needs to help search". A better sentence is: "Search and Platform are collaborating for six weeks to discover the deployment interface; after that the capability should be consumed as a service."

## Rationale

Many software organisations use the word "team" for very different things: a product team, a component group, a guild, a project group, a support queue, an architecture board, a platform team, or a reporting line. The ambiguity hides real design choices.

Team Topologies gives Organization a small language for those choices. It makes the organisation ask:

* Which team owns this stream of value?
* Which dependencies are unavoidable, and which are design debt?
* Which capabilities should be consumed as a product?
* Which teams need temporary help instead of permanent handoff?
* Which subsystem is genuinely too cognitively heavy for a stream-aligned team?

This reinforces [[small-and-simple]], [[capabilities-follow-ownership]], and [[standardize-before-diverging]]. The goal is not terminology purity. The goal is fast flow through team boundaries that people can understand.

## Team Type Guardrails

| Team type | Valid when | Failure mode |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Stream-aligned | The team can own outcomes and systems for a coherent value stream. | Becomes a feature factory with no product context. |
| Platform | The team has internal customers, product thinking, self-service capabilities, and support paths. | Becomes a ticket-driven infrastructure silo. |
| Enabling | The team has a temporary mission to grow capability in other teams. | Becomes a permanent dependency or consulting bottleneck. |
| Complicated-subsystem | The subsystem needs deep expertise and has a clear API to stream teams. | Becomes a component team that forces constant coordination. |

No team should be labelled as a platform or enabling team just because that sounds modern. The label must match how the team behaves.

## Interaction Mode Guardrails

Interaction modes should appear in team APIs, planning notes, ADRs, and cross-team working agreements.

| Mode | Healthy signal | Warning signal |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Collaboration | Time-boxed, high-bandwidth joint work around uncertainty. | Permanent meetings with no boundary decision. |
| X-as-a-service | Clear interface, support route, documentation, lifecycle, and adoption path. | Hidden Slack support and tribal knowledge. |
| Facilitating | The receiving team grows capability and needs less help over time. | The enabling team keeps doing the work indefinitely. |

When the mode is unclear, people invent their own expectations. That is how "just ask platform" turns into a queue and "architecture advice" turns into a hidden approval gate.

## Worked Example

A stream-aligned team wants to adopt a new observability standard.

Possible topology interpretation:

| Stage | Interaction mode | Why |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Early discovery | Collaboration | The standard is not mature enough; the service team and platform team need to learn together. |
| Rollout | Facilitating | The observability enabling group helps the team instrument services and dashboards. |
| Steady state | X-as-a-service | Dashboards, alert templates, tracing libraries, and documentation are stable platform capabilities. |

The same two teams can move through several modes. Trouble starts when everyone assumes a different mode: platform thinks it is offering a service, the product team expects hands-on help, and leadership thinks rollout is finished.

## Operating Rhythm

Engineering leadership should review team topology at least quarterly and whenever major strategy changes occur.

Useful review prompts:

* Which teams have unclear or overloaded missions?
* Which teams are carrying too much cognitive load?
* Which platform capabilities are still ticket queues?
* Which enabling engagements should end, continue, or become self-service?
* Which dependencies should move from collaboration to X-as-a-service?
* Which complicated subsystems are justified, and which are accidental component teams?

The review output should be recorded as decisions or working agreements, not as private org-chart commentary.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Renamed silo.** An infrastructure group calls itself a platform team but still operates as a ticket queue.
* **Permanent facilitation.** An enabling team keeps taking work from other teams instead of growing capability.
* **Component maze.** Every customer change requires coordination across many narrow component teams.
* **Topology theatre.** Team Topologies vocabulary appears in slides but not in team APIs, ownership, or planning.
* **Unbounded collaboration.** Teams collaborate forever because no one decides the future boundary.

## Discussion Prompts

Use these prompts during topology reviews:

* Which teams are stream-aligned, platform, enabling, or complicated-subsystem teams today?
* Which team labels do not match actual behaviour?
* Which interactions should be collaboration, service consumption, or facilitation?
* Which dependencies create the most waiting or rework?
* Which platform capabilities are still handled through tickets or private chat?
* Which enabling engagements should now end, change mode, or become self-service?

## Scope and Revisiting

This decision applies to engineering organisation design, not to HR reporting lines alone. A reporting line may contain multiple team types; a team type may require collaboration across reporting lines.

Revisit if the vocabulary stops improving clarity, if platform/enabling labels are abused, or if Organization adopts a different explicit org-design model.
