> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization will use explicit guardrails for team size and composition. A healthy product engineering team normally has five to eight engineers, one clear product partner, design access where user experience matters, technical leadership, and enough delivery, quality, data, and operational skill to own its stream. Teams below four engineers are fragile; teams above ten core contributors should usually split, narrow scope, or change topology.

## Decision

Adopt team size and composition guardrails for stream-aligned product teams.

The normal target is:

| Dimension | Default guardrail |
| --- | --- |
| Core engineers | 5-8 engineers |
| Total standing team | Usually 6-10 people including product/design/QE roles |
| Product ownership | One named product partner or equivalent decision owner |
| Technical leadership | One named tech lead role, not necessarily a people manager |
| Quality ownership | Quality skills embedded in the team, with QE enablement where needed |
| Operational ownership | The team can deploy, observe, support, and improve its systems |
| Bus factor | No critical capability depends on one person indefinitely |

The guardrails are not a spreadsheet rule. They are prompts for leadership judgement. The point is to protect team cognition, resilience, and ownership.

## How to Read This Decision

| What it says | What it does **not** say |
| --- | --- |
| Most teams should be small and durable. | Every team must have exactly the same headcount. |
| Skill mix matters as much as size. | Every skill must be represented by a full-time dedicated person. |
| Teams below four engineers are organisationally fragile. | Small temporary groups can never exist. |
| Teams above ten core contributors need review. | Large missions must always be split immediately. |
| Composition follows ownership. | People should be copied into every team just in case. |

Team design is a socio-technical decision: the shape of the team should match the product boundary, system boundary, and cognitive load.

## How to Use This Decision

Use this decision to:

* explain why team size affects ownership, learning, and delivery flow;
* tell the difference between headcount capacity and real team capability;
* identify missing skills in a product team without defaulting to fractional staffing;
* recognise when a team should be split, narrowed, or supported by platform/enabling work;
* use team-shape discussions as design conversations, not only budget conversations.

The useful question is not "how many people are on the team?" The useful question is "can this group sustainably own its mission with the skills, context, and authority it has?"

## One-Minute Mental Model

Team size has two failure cliffs.

Below the lower cliff, the team is fragile: support rotation, holidays, incidents, reviews, and onboarding all depend on too few people. Above the upper cliff, the team is noisy: coordination cost grows, internal subgroups form, and ownership becomes vague.

Composition is the second half of the model. Eight engineers with no product context or quality strategy are not a healthy product team. A nominally cross-functional team made of many 10 percent allocations is also not healthy. A real team needs enough dedicated people and enough reliable skills to own the work.

## Rationale

Very small teams move quickly for a while, then become fragile. They struggle with support rotation, pairing, reviews, vacations, incidents, onboarding, and succession. Very large teams preserve capacity on paper while losing shared context in practice. Coordination cost rises, meetings multiply, and ownership blurs.

The target range gives teams enough people to sustain delivery and operations without creating an internal bureaucracy. It also makes team responsibilities legible: if a team needs many more people, either the mission is too broad, the system is too complex, or the team is doing work that should be moved to a platform, enabling, or complicated-subsystem topology.

Composition is equally important. A team with eight backend engineers and no product access, user insight, quality strategy, or operational capability is not cross-functional. A team with every specialist assigned at 10 percent is also not cross-functional; it is a meeting schedule.

## Minimum Viable Composition

A stream-aligned team should be able to answer these questions without routing every decision elsewhere:

| Capability | Minimum expectation |
| --- | --- |
| Product direction | Who decides priorities and interprets product strategy for this stream? |
| User and customer understanding | Who brings recent evidence about users, customers, partners, or internal consumers? |
| Engineering delivery | Who can design, implement, review, release, and maintain the system? |
| Technical leadership | Who keeps tradeoffs explicit and raises cross-team decisions early? |
| Quality strategy | Who designs the test approach, risk approach, and release confidence model? |
| Operations | Who can respond to incidents, understand observability, and improve runbooks? |
| Data and analytics | Who knows whether the product is producing the intended outcome? |

Some capabilities may come through part-time collaboration or enabling support. The important point is that the team has reliable access and clear ownership, not that every role appears as a permanent full-time job title.

## Composition Patterns

Useful starting patterns:

| Team situation | Likely composition |
| --- | --- |
| User-facing product stream | PM, product designer, tech lead, 5-8 engineers, QE skill, analytics access. |
| Platform product stream | Platform product owner, tech lead, platform engineers, developer-experience feedback path, reliability/security input. |
| High-risk regulated or legacy stream | Stronger QE presence, operational expertise, architecture advice, and explicit risk review. |
| Early discovery stream | Smaller core group with product, design, tech lead, and one or two engineers; expand only when delivery starts. |
| Migration stream | Temporary cross-functional group with a clear retirement date and receiving owners. |

Avoid solving every shortage by adding fractional people. Too many fractional assignments increase coordination load and create weak accountability.

## Sizing Smells

Use these smells to decide whether a team needs design attention:

| Smell | Likely interpretation | Better question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Two engineers own a production service with on-call. | The team is under-resilient. | What is the minimum sustainable support model? |
| Twelve people attend every planning meeting. | The mission may be too broad or internally split. | Which value streams or subdomains are hidden inside the team? |
| A specialist attends five teams' ceremonies. | The role is spread too thin to be useful. | Should this skill be embedded, enabled, or turned into a platform capability? |
| The team cannot release without another team. | Ownership boundary or platform capability is incomplete. | Is the dependency temporary, service-like, or a sign of wrong team boundaries? |
| One person reviews every important change. | Knowledge and decision authority are concentrated. | How do we grow reviewers and reduce single-person bottlenecks? |

Sizing problems are often symptoms. The cause may be architecture, product scope, platform weakness, unclear ownership, or leadership overcommitment.

## What This Means for Leaders

Engineering and product leaders should review team shape when:

* a team is smaller than four engineers for more than one quarter;
* a team has more than ten regular contributors;
* support load repeatedly blocks product work;
* product discovery happens outside the team;
* releases require routine coordination across many component teams;
* one person is the bottleneck for a critical skill;
* a team depends on specialists who are never available when decisions are made.

The first fix is not always "add people". Often the better answer is narrowing the mission, simplifying the system, moving a shared capability to a platform team, or changing interaction mode.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Two-person product team.** Fast until someone is sick, on holiday, or leaves.
* **Fifteen-person standup.** Capacity looks high, but nobody can hold the whole mission in their head.
* **Fractional specialist swarm.** Everyone is assigned to the team, nobody is truly available to it.
* **Missing product partner.** Engineers receive tickets but not product context or outcome ownership.
* **Missing operations skill.** The team builds software it cannot confidently run.
* **Single hero skill.** A critical system depends on one irreplaceable person.

## Discussion Prompts

Use these prompts when reviewing a team:

* Which responsibilities would fail if one person left?
* Which skills does the team need all the time, and which can be accessed through enabling or platform support?
* Where does the team wait for someone outside the team before it can release?
* Is the team large because the mission is broad, the system is complex, or demand is uncontrolled?
* Are fractional roles helping the team, or only making the org chart look complete?
* What would we remove from the team's mission before adding more people?

## Scope and Revisiting

This ADR applies to standing product engineering teams. It does not prescribe the size of incident response groups, discovery spikes, migration tiger teams, guilds, or leadership groups.

Revisit the guardrails if the organisation collects evidence that different size ranges are consistently healthier for its actual domains and constraints.
