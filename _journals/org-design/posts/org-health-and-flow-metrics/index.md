> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization will use a balanced engineering health and flow measurement system. Product outcomes, delivery flow, reliability, quality, developer experience, and team health are reviewed together. Metrics are used to improve the system of work, not to rank individuals, maximise activity, or create a single productivity score.

## Decision

Adopt an **organisation health and flow metrics** model.

The model combines:

* product and business outcome measures;
* DORA-style software delivery and stability metrics;
* reliability and operational health signals;
* quality signals from testing, defects, incidents, and customer feedback;
* developer experience and cognitive-load signals;
* team health and collaboration signals.

No single metric represents engineering productivity. The operating rhythm should review a small set of signals together, interpret them in context, and turn learning into actions.

## How to Read This Decision

| What it says | What it does **not** say |
| --- | --- |
| Metrics should guide learning and improvement. | Metrics should rank individual engineers. |
| Delivery flow and stability both matter. | Faster deployment is always better. |
| Developer experience is a real management concern. | Feelings replace product and operational evidence. |
| Team health should be visible. | Surveys are enough without follow-up action. |
| Use a balanced set of signals. | Build a giant dashboard nobody acts on. |

The point is management attention, not measurement theatre.

## How to Use This Decision

Use this decision to:

* explain why engineering productivity cannot be reduced to one number;
* distinguish product outcomes, delivery flow, stability, quality, developer experience, and team health;
* choose metrics that create learning rather than fear or gaming;
* interpret team metrics in context instead of comparing teams mechanically;
* turn a metrics review into an improvement decision.

The main lesson is that metrics are instruments for seeing the system. They are dangerous when used as individual surveillance or as substitutes for judgement.

## One-Minute Mental Model

Engineering metrics are a **balanced dashboard for a socio-technical system**.

Product outcomes tell whether work matters. Flow metrics tell whether change moves through the system. Stability and quality metrics tell whether change is safe. Developer experience and team health tell whether the system is sustainable.

No category is enough alone. A team can deploy often and still build the wrong thing. A team can have low incident count because it rarely changes anything. A team can close many tickets while burning out. Interpretation matters.

## Rationale

Engineering leaders need evidence, but the wrong evidence damages the system. Counting commits, lines of code, tickets closed, or story points encourages local activity and gaming. Teams become busy while outcomes, quality, and sustainability degrade.

Modern engineering metrics must respect that software work is socio-technical. Flow depends on architecture, dependencies, review speed, cognitive load, deployment paths, team health, product clarity, and operational feedback. A balanced model makes those constraints visible.

This ADR aligns with [[observable-by-default]], [[production-ready]], and [[architecture-as-advice-and-guardrails]]. If the organisation cannot observe its own delivery system, it cannot improve it responsibly.

## Metric Categories

| Category | Example signals | Primary question |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Product outcomes | Adoption, conversion, retention, task success, internal-customer satisfaction. | Are we creating the intended value? |
| Delivery flow | Lead time for changes, deployment frequency, WIP, dependency wait time. | Can teams move changes safely through the system? |
| Stability and reliability | Change fail rate, failed deployment recovery time, SLOs, incidents, alert quality. | Are users protected from change and operational failure? |
| Quality | Escaped defects, flaky tests, automated confidence, support contacts, rework. | Are we building and validating the right level of quality? |
| Developer experience | Feedback-loop time, cognitive load, friction, tool satisfaction, focus time. | Can engineers do good work without unnecessary drag? |
| Team health | Clarity, trust, role balance, decision quality, collaboration, learning cadence. | Is the team sustainable and effective? |

The organisation should choose a small number of actionable signals per category rather than collecting everything.

## Operating Rhythm

Metrics should be reviewed at three levels:

| Level | Cadence | Focus |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Team | Weekly to monthly | Local flow, quality, incidents, team health actions, product learning. |
| Domain / group | Monthly to quarterly | Cross-team dependencies, platform friction, reliability trends, staffing gaps. |
| Engineering leadership | Quarterly | Strategy, investment, capability gaps, systemic impediments, portfolio tradeoffs. |

Every review should end with decisions or experiments:

* What did we learn?
* Which constraint matters most now?
* What will we change?
* Who owns the follow-up?
* When will we revisit?

If a metric produces no action over time, remove it.

## Worked Example

Leadership sees that deployment frequency is low in one domain.

Bad interpretation:

* Tell teams to deploy more often.
* Compare the domain unfavourably with another domain.
* Ask managers to report weekly deployment counts.

Better interpretation:

| Signal to inspect | Possible learning |
| --- | --- |
| Lead time | Work may wait in review, testing, dependency, or approval queues. |
| Change fail rate | Teams may be avoiding deployments because releases are risky. |
| Failed deployment recovery time | Rollback, observability, or incident response may be weak. |
| Developer experience | Tooling, environments, or cognitive load may be slowing delivery. |
| Product context | Work may be bundled because planning creates large batches. |

The response might be smaller batches, better test confidence, platform pipeline improvements, clearer ownership, or product planning changes. The metric opens the investigation; it does not dictate the answer.

## Guardrails Against Harmful Use

Metrics must not be used to:

* rank individual engineers by activity;
* compare teams without context;
* punish teams for surfacing problems;
* maximise one dimension while damaging another;
* replace judgement with dashboards;
* hide product or organisational problems behind engineering productivity language.

Any metric used for performance management must be reviewed carefully. Most engineering flow metrics are safer as team and system signals than as individual evaluation data.

## Team Health

Team health should be assessed explicitly. Useful prompts include:

* Do we understand our mission and users?
* Do we have the skills and roles needed for our ownership?
* Are our decision rights clear?
* Are dependencies manageable?
* Can we release and operate confidently?
* Do we have enough focus time?
* Do we learn from incidents and defects?
* Is the team sustainable?

Health checks are only useful if they create action. Repeatedly collecting low scores without changing the system teaches cynicism.

## Anti-Patterns

* **One productivity number.** Leadership asks for a single metric that ranks teams or individuals.
* **Activity worship.** Commits, tickets, or story points are mistaken for value.
* **Dashboard graveyard.** Metrics are collected but never interpreted or acted on.
* **Context-free comparison.** A platform team, migration team, and product team are compared as if their work were identical.
* **Metric fear.** Teams hide defects, incidents, or slowdowns because metrics are used punitively.
* **Local optimisation.** Lead time improves while reliability, quality, or team health degrades.

## Discussion Prompts

Use these prompts in metrics reviews:

* Which outcome are we trying to improve, and which metric is only a proxy?
* What behaviour could this metric accidentally encourage?
* What context is needed before comparing this signal across teams?
* Which metric moved, and what changed in the system of work?
* What decision or experiment follows from this review?
* Which metric should we stop tracking because nobody acts on it?

## Scope and Revisiting

This ADR applies to engineering leadership, product engineering teams, platform teams, and communities responsible for engineering practice.

Revisit if metrics are used punitively, if dashboards grow without action, if teams game measures, or if leadership cannot see the main constraints in engineering flow.
