> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization will treat engineering management as a distinct leadership discipline. Engineering managers are accountable for people growth, team health, hiring, performance, delivery systems, organisational clarity, and sustainable execution. They partner with product and technical leaders; they do not replace product ownership, technical leadership, or project administration.

## Decision

Define the **engineering management operating model**.

Engineering managers are accountable for:

* building and maintaining healthy teams;
* coaching and developing engineers;
* hiring, onboarding, performance, and progression;
* ensuring teams have clear missions, roles, and working agreements;
* improving the delivery system and removing organisational impediments;
* partnering with product and design leadership on outcomes and priorities;
* ensuring technical health, operational health, and quality have real capacity;
* escalating unresolved constraints with evidence.

Engineering managers are not the default owner of every technical decision. They ensure the right people can make and record those decisions.

## How to Read This Decision

| What it says | What it does **not** say |
| --- | --- |
| Engineering management is a profession. | Strong engineers naturally know how to manage. |
| EMs own team health and delivery systems. | EMs personally manage every task and deadline. |
| EMs partner with product and tech leads. | EMs replace product managers or tech leads. |
| EMs need technical judgement. | EMs must be the primary architect. |
| EM span of control should be healthy. | Every reporting line has the same number of people. |

The EM role exists to make sustained team ownership possible.

## How to Use This Decision

Use this decision to:

* explain what engineering managers are accountable for beyond project tracking;
* distinguish management work from product ownership and technical leadership;
* recognise when autonomy has become neglect or management has become micromanagement;
* reason about healthy span of control;
* use the EM/PM/tech-lead partnership to clarify decisions and tradeoffs.

The main learning is that good management is not command-and-control. It is the deliberate design and maintenance of the people system that lets teams own outcomes sustainably.

## One-Minute Mental Model

Engineering management is **stewardship of the team system**.

The manager does not need to make every product or technical decision. The manager makes sure the team has mission clarity, staffing, skills, feedback, focus, accountability, and a healthy delivery system. When those conditions are missing, autonomy becomes abandonment.

The strongest EMs create teams that can make better decisions without routing every decision through the manager.

## Rationale

Modern engineering teams need autonomy, but autonomy without management becomes neglect. Teams need clarity, feedback, staffing, performance handling, prioritisation discipline, and protection from overload. Those are management responsibilities.

At the same time, management should not collapse into project chasing. If the EM becomes the person assigning every ticket, approving every technical choice, and negotiating every delivery detail, the team loses ownership and the EM becomes a bottleneck.

The operating model separates leadership concerns:

* product leadership clarifies value and priority;
* technical leadership clarifies technical tradeoffs and system direction;
* engineering management clarifies team capability, delivery health, people growth, and organisational constraints.

The three roles overlap through partnership, not role confusion.

## EM Responsibilities

| Responsibility | Good evidence |
| --- | --- |
| Team health | Regular 1:1s, retrospectives, health checks, psychological safety, manageable load. |
| Capability growth | Growth plans, mentoring, hiring plan, skill gaps visible and acted on. |
| Delivery system | Flow impediments identified, WIP controlled, dependencies managed, planning realistic. |
| Performance | Expectations clear, feedback timely, underperformance handled directly and fairly. |
| Product partnership | Priorities understood, tradeoffs explicit, scope/time/quality decisions visible. |
| Technical health | Technical debt and reliability work funded, not hidden behind feature delivery. |
| Organisational clarity | Team mission, ownership, dependencies, and decision rights are clear. |

The EM does not need to solve every item personally. The EM is accountable for ensuring the system handles them.

## Span of Control

Healthy management span depends on maturity, domain complexity, seniority mix, and amount of change. As a starting guardrail:

| Situation | Suggested span |
| --- | --- |
| New manager, new team, high change, many juniors | 4-6 direct reports |
| Stable team with mixed seniority | 6-8 direct reports |
| Very senior, stable group with strong tech leads | 8-10 direct reports may be possible |
| Manager of managers | Fewer direct reports, higher organisational scope |

When span is too large, management becomes reactive. Career development and performance feedback degrade first, then team health, then delivery quality.

## EM, PM, and Tech Lead Partnership

The core leadership triangle:

| Role | Owns primarily | Must partner on |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Product partner | Product value, user/customer outcomes, priority, stakeholder context. | Feasibility, quality, team capacity, discovery, delivery tradeoffs. |
| Engineering manager | Team health, people, capability, delivery system, staffing. | Priority, technical health, incidents, planning, performance expectations. |
| Tech lead / senior IC | Technical direction, design quality, engineering tradeoffs, local architecture. | Product outcome, team skill growth, delivery sequencing, operational health. |

No role should become a proxy for the others. If the PM is absent, the EM should not quietly become product owner. If the tech lead is absent, the EM should not become permanent architect by default.

## Worked Example

A team is missing delivery commitments and quality is slipping.

Project-manager reaction:

* Ask for more detailed status updates.
* Push engineers to commit harder.
* Escalate delays late.
* Treat quality work as a competing backlog item.

Engineering-management reaction:

| Question | Management action |
| --- | --- |
| Is the team overloaded? | Review WIP, support load, unplanned work, and priority conflicts. |
| Is the mission clear? | Reconfirm outcomes, tradeoffs, and decision rights with product and tech lead. |
| Is capability missing? | Identify skill gaps, mentoring needs, hiring needs, or platform support. |
| Is technical debt blocking flow? | Make technical health visible in planning and leadership tradeoff discussions. |
| Is feedback healthy? | Use retrospectives, 1:1s, and metrics to identify systemic blockers. |

The EM still cares about delivery. The difference is that delivery failure is treated as a system signal, not only an individual effort problem.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Project-manager EM.** The manager tracks tasks but does not coach people, improve systems, or handle performance.
* **Chief architect EM.** Every technical decision routes through the manager.
* **Absentee autonomy.** The team is told it is autonomous but receives no coaching, context, or impediment removal.
* **Too many reports.** The manager has so many people that 1:1s, feedback, and growth become symbolic.
* **Priority shield failure.** The EM lets uncontrolled demand overload the team and then treats burnout as motivation.
* **Career path confusion.** Engineers become managers without understanding the change in work.

## Discussion Prompts

Use these prompts in EM self-reviews or leadership reviews:

* What part of the team's system most limits sustainable delivery right now?
* Are 1:1s producing growth and feedback, or only status?
* Which team decisions route through the manager unnecessarily?
* Which people issues are being avoided because delivery pressure is high?
* Does the team have enough technical leadership and product partnership?
* Is the manager's span still compatible with real coaching and performance work?

## Scope and Revisiting

This ADR applies to engineering management roles and their partnership with product, design, quality, platform, and senior technical roles.

Revisit if managers are overloaded, if teams lack clarity, if technical decisions route through managers by default, or if career progression keeps pushing engineers into management against their strengths.
