> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Organization engineering will simplify and converge its current landscape to improve delivery speed and operational stability. Optimise for Germany-scale needs, not for inherited White-Label-scale optionality. That means fewer active patterns, fewer tooling paths, clearer ownership, explicit legacy retirement, and a simpler path from code to production. The near-term focus is delivery-path simplification, ownership clarity, backend convergence, and containment of infrastructure fragmentation.

## Decision

Organization engineering should optimise for the business it actually has - a stable single-brand operation for the next 2-3 years - and stop optimising by default for the multi-brand, multi-country platform scale that shaped much of the current estate.

That single re-framing leads to the rest of the strategy:

* The main strategic problem is **fragmentation**, not lack of technical sophistication.
* The main strategic move is **simplification and convergence**, not adding more architectural variety.
* The main strategic burden is **inherited complexity**, made up of White-Label-era platform ambitions layered on top of older legacy that was never retired.
* The main strategic outcome is **faster delivery and more stable operations**, measured by delivery speed, stability, quality, onboarding, and ownership clarity.

Everything else in this journal - diagnosis, policy, operating model, blueprints, roadmap - is downstream of this re-framing.

## The Single Most Load-Bearing Sentence

> Organization is carrying more engineering complexity than its current business needs justify.

Every other claim in this strategy is downstream of that one. If that sentence is wrong, the strategy is wrong. If that sentence is right, the implications follow almost mechanically.

The sentence rests on four observations:

1. Germany is expected to be a **stable single-brand business** for the next 2-3 years.
2. The current estate carries **White-Label-scale optionality** designed for many brands and countries.
3. **Older legacy was never fully removed** when newer eras were introduced.
4. The result is **three architectural eras coexisting** with no clear target state, fragmented delivery tooling, and roughly 200 AWS accounts.

The full evidence base is in [[diagnosis]].

## Policy in One Page

A complete restatement of the policy lives in [[policy]]. The one-page version is:

### Objective

Increase delivery speed and stability by reducing fragmentation.

### Direction

* Optimise for Germany-scale needs, not inherited White-Label optionality.
* Simplify and converge rather than add more architectural sophistication.
* Reduce tool, platform, and infrastructure variance.
* Clarify ownership boundaries.
* Retire or contain legacy explicitly.
* Standardise the path from code to production.

### Core Policies

1. Simplification and convergence are primary objectives.
2. Backend convergence is the main technical direction.
3. Delivery-path simplification is part of the core strategy.
4. Legacy retirement is mandatory.
5. Ownership boundaries must become clear.
6. The number of supported technologies and toolchains must decrease over time.
7. Strong defaults are preferred over broad optionality.
8. New fragmentation requires explicit justification.
9. Success is measured by delivery speed, stability, quality, onboarding, and ownership clarity.

### Decision Rules

When choosing between options, prefer the option that:

1. Reduces fragmentation.
2. Fits Germany's actual scope.
3. Simplifies delivery.
4. Clarifies ownership.
5. Creates a path to retire legacy.

Reject options that add durable complexity without clear Germany-specific value.

## What the Strategy Does Not Try to Do

The strategy is deliberately narrow. To stay actionable, it does **not** try to:

| Not in scope | Reason |
| --- | --- |
| Specify the exact target architecture in detail | That is the job of the blueprints, evolved over time. |
| Pick a single backend technology for every domain | Convergence reduces *variance*; it does not name one runtime as the universal answer. |
| Schedule team-by-team migrations | Sequencing belongs to the roadmap and wave plan, not to the policy. |
| Replace the AVIV Group AWS Foundation or White-Label golden paths | Those are starting points; divergence requires explicit justification, not blanket invention. |
| Become an architecture committee | A lightweight review forum applies the strategy; it does not re-litigate every decision. |
| Resolve every open question now | The strategy is published with explicit open questions and a review cadence. |

The strategy is a frame, not a finished design.

## Current Shape of the Estate

The starting position, in numbers and patterns:

* Roughly **200 AWS accounts**, split between a legacy account estate and a White-Label-era account estate.
* More than **2,000 repositories** across GitHub, GitLab, and TFS-era systems.
* Roughly **30 million lines of code**.
* Delivery fragmented across **GitHub, CircleCI, GitLab, TFS, Jenkins, and manual paths**.
* **Three backend styles in parallel:**
  * Monolith on SQL Server and C#.
  * Event-driven Java microservices on self-hosted Kafka.
  * Serverless-based newer services.
* Active legacy technologies still in the estate: self-hosted Kafka, RabbitMQ, SQL Server, Windows servers, Java, ASPX, GitLab, Jenkins, Team Foundation Server, Silverlight, Akamai.

These numbers are not failures. They are the consequence of a multi-year White-Label platform ambition layered on top of older legacy. The strategy treats them as a starting point, not an indictment.

## Load-Bearing Reasoning

The strategy rests on four pieces of reasoning. Each is challengeable; if any one of them changes, the strategy should be revisited.

1. **Germany is a stable single-brand business for the next 2-3 years.** If multi-brand or multi-country requirements return on a shorter horizon, the optionality this strategy proposes to remove may need to be preserved.
2. **The current estate carries White-Label-scale and legacy-era complexity.** If the diagnosis is wrong about *where* the complexity comes from, the prescription will treat the wrong root cause.
3. **The main business need is faster delivery and stability, not maximum architectural flexibility.** If the business shifts to optimise for, e.g., new product exploration over delivery speed, the policy should weight differently.
4. **The main problem is fragmentation, not lack of technical sophistication.** If the diagnosis is wrong about this, the strategy will simplify in places that did not need simplifying and leave the real bottleneck untouched.

These are stated explicitly so they can be argued with.

## Alternatives Not Chosen

The strategy explicitly rejects several plausible alternative directions:

| Alternative | Why not |
| --- | --- |
| Continue optimising by default for multi-brand, multi-country platform scale | Mismatches the current business shape; preserves the White-Label-era complexity that is now a delivery tax. |
| Treat serverless-first architecture as the default backend direction | Adds another backend style on top of the three already in play; increases variance instead of reducing it. |
| Accept mixed-generation delivery tooling as a normal steady state | Directly contradicts the business expectation of faster delivery and stability. |
| Modernise by adding new layers while leaving old ones active without containment or retirement | This is exactly the pattern that produced the current estate; doing more of it makes the problem worse. |
| Do a full top-down redesign | Higher risk than the current diagnosis warrants; incremental convergence is more likely to actually reduce active complexity. |

## What "Success" Looks Like

The strategy is not about architectural elegance. It is about whether the engineering system works better for Germany. The signals to watch:

* The number of active backend patterns is **decreasing**, not increasing.
* The number of active delivery paths is **decreasing**, not increasing.
* The supported technology and tooling set is **shrinking** over time.
* The AWS account estate has **explicit owner and status** for every account; unclear-purpose accounts are reducing.
* **Onboarding** time and effort for new engineers is decreasing.
* **Ownership** of major systems is clear, named, and accountable.
* **Delivery speed and operational stability** trend the right way.

If, after a year, the active footprint has grown rather than shrunk, the strategy has not worked - regardless of how many new initiatives have started.

## Anti-Patterns

The strategy fails in predictable ways. Watch for:

* **Treating simplification as background cleanup.** If "simplify when you have time" is the operating mode, the policy is not being applied.
* **Adding new platforms on top of old ones without containment or retirement.** The fastest way to recreate the current diagnosis.
* **Letting the review forum become an architecture committee.** It exists to review decisions with strategic impact, not to gate every change.
* **Counting plans as progress.** Retirement plans without retirements do not reduce active complexity.
* **Justifying local preference with strategic language.** "Strategic" is not a synonym for "what this team wanted to do anyway".

## Scope and Revisiting

This strategy is written for a 2-3 year horizon. Revisit it materially when one of the following changes:

* The business scope of Organization Germany changes (multi-brand, multi-country, divestiture, major acquisition).
* The organisation structure changes in a way that affects platform ownership.
* A major delivery tooling decision is made centrally elsewhere in AVIV Group.
* The diagnosis - that fragmentation, not sophistication, is the bottleneck - turns out to be wrong.

In the absence of those triggers, revisit policy and roadmap quarterly through the operating mechanisms, not by rewriting this document.
