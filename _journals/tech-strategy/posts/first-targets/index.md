> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Concentrate the first wave of simplification on five target groups: delivery path fragmentation, legacy backend and integration surfaces, the AWS account estate, the legacy technology footprint, and ownership hotspots. Each is chosen for high delivery friction, high ownership confusion, high operational burden, high fragmentation, and a realistic path to containment, consolidation, or retirement. The post identifies target areas and immediate questions; project plans and dates come from the wave plan in [[prioritization]].

## Decision

This post answers the question "**where do we start?**". It is the bridge between [[roadmap]] (which sequences the strategy by phase) and [[prioritization]] (which scores and waves specific candidates).

The post does not yet commit to:

* Named systems.
* Dates.
* Budgets.
* Project plans.

What it does commit to: **the first execution focus should be these five target groups**.

## How to Use These Targets

Use this record to focus discovery and first-wave planning. Each target group should produce:

* A small inventory of concrete candidates.
* A named owner or owner-finding action.
* A first estimate of delivery friction, stability risk, ownership confusion, fragmentation impact, and ease of action.
* A decision on whether the candidate belongs in Wave 1, Wave 2, or the Investigation Queue in [[prioritization]].
* A clear statement of what active complexity would be removed, contained, or made visible.

Do not treat the five target groups as five programmes. They are areas where the first measurable simplification candidates should be found.

## What This Should Help You Do

This record should help a reader:

* Explain why the first wave should focus on delivery path, backend and integration surfaces, AWS accounts, legacy footprint, and ownership hotspots.
* Turn a broad target group into concrete candidates for scoring.
* Separate discovery work from execution work.
* Avoid treating every strategic theme as a simultaneous programme.

Common misreadings to avoid:

| Misreading | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| "These five groups are the whole strategy." | They are first targets, not the full strategy. |
| "Every group should start as a large project." | Each group should produce candidates, then prioritization decides waves. |
| "First means easiest." | First means high-friction, high-value, and realistic enough to act on. |

## Selection Logic

The first targets are chosen where there is a combination of:

* **High delivery friction** - teams feel the cost daily.
* **High ownership confusion** - decisions are blocked because no one is accountable.
* **High operational burden** - operations spend disproportionate effort here.
* **High fragmentation** - many parallel patterns or estates.
* **Realistic path to containment, consolidation, or retirement** - meaningful progress is possible in this strategy horizon.

Targets that score high on the first four but have no realistic path to action stay in the **Investigate** queue (see [[prioritization]]) until that path becomes clearer.

## Target Group 1. Delivery Path Fragmentation

### Why this is first

Delivery speed and stability are **explicit business expectations** (see [[diagnosis]]), and the current delivery landscape is visibly fragmented across several generations of tooling and process.

### First focus areas

* **Manual build paths.**
* **Manual deployment paths.**
* **Legacy CI/CD flows with no clear long-term place.**
* **Duplicate deployment paths** for similar runtime classes.

### Desired outcome

* Fewer ways to build and deploy.
* Clearer default path from code to production.
* Less operational dependency on tribal knowledge.

### Immediate questions

* Which delivery paths are still manual?
* Which pipelines have no named owner?
* Which runtime classes can share **one** standard delivery path?

Detail: [[delivery-path]] for blueprint and convergence plan.

## Target Group 2. Legacy Backend and Integration Surfaces

### Why this is first

Backend fragmentation is the **strongest architectural mismatch** in the [[diagnosis]] (implication 4 in [[implications]]).

### First focus areas

* Services or components that still rely on **shared-database coupling**.
* Backend areas where **monolith, event-driven services, and serverless overlap** without clear reason.
* **Legacy messaging patterns** that remain active only because no retirement decision has been made.
* Backend components that create **repeated ownership or operational confusion**.

### Desired outcome

* Clearer backend default direction.
* Fewer overlapping paradigms.
* More explicit service and data ownership.

### Immediate questions

* Which backend areas currently combine multiple paradigms?
* Where is hidden coupling highest?
* Which integrations can move to simpler, clearer contracts first?

Detail: [[backend-convergence]] for blueprint and convergence plan.

## Target Group 3. AWS Account Estate

### Why this is first

The account footprint is **one of the clearest visible signs of structural fragmentation** in the [[diagnosis]]: roughly 200 accounts, split between a legacy estate and a White-Label-era estate, with a long tail of transitional or unclear-purpose accounts.

### First focus areas

* **Accounts with unclear owner.**
* **Accounts with unclear purpose.**
* **Transitional accounts that have become permanent.**
* **Duplicated environment structures** with limited strategic value.

### Desired outcome

* Explicit ownership and status for the estate.
* First candidates for consolidation or retirement.
* Account creation under tighter control.

### Immediate questions

* Which accounts are active, transitional, or legacy?
* Which accounts have no clear accountable owner?
* Which account boundaries no longer reflect real operational needs?

Detail: [[aws-accounts]] for blueprint and consolidation plan.

## Target Group 4. Legacy Technology Footprint

### Why this is first

The [[diagnosis]] shows that Germany still carries multiple generations of technologies and tools, many of which survived prior migrations **without a clear strategic role**.

### First focus areas

* Technologies with **high operational burden and low strategic value**.
* Tools with **overlapping purpose** across the same lifecycle stage.
* **Obsolete platforms** kept active only because of historical inertia.

Examples already visible in the diagnosis:

* **Jenkins** and the mixed-generation CI/CD estate.
* **TFS-era tooling.**
* **Self-hosted Kafka and RabbitMQ** overlap.
* **Old Windows-based runtime surfaces.**
* **ASPX and Silverlight** legacy surfaces.

### Desired outcome

* A smaller supported technology set.
* Explicit containment or retirement status for legacy tools and runtimes.
* Fewer technologies that new engineers must understand.

### Immediate questions

* Which technologies are **strategic**?
* Which are **tolerated legacy**?
* Which have the **highest operational cost relative to business value**?

The distinction between "strategic" and "tolerated legacy" is load-bearing. Tolerated legacy is not the strategic default. Conflating them is one of the [[operate]] anti-patterns.

## Target Group 5. Ownership Hotspots

### Why this is first

Unclear ownership is one of the most visible consequences in the [[diagnosis]] and directly affects quality, onboarding, and delivery. It is also a **prerequisite** for the deeper convergence work in later roadmap phases - see [[roadmap]] Phase 2 → Phase 3 dependency.

### First focus areas

* Systems with **shared or disputed ownership**.
* Platform surfaces with **no clear decision-maker**.
* Legacy areas that **everyone depends on but no one truly owns**.
* Places where **team and system boundaries no longer match**.

### Desired outcome

* Named owners for major systems and legacy estates.
* Clearer accountability for simplification and retirement decisions.
* Less ambiguity in operational responsibility.

### Immediate questions

* Which systems have shared ownership today?
* Which systems create repeated coordination problems?
* Where does lack of ownership block simplification?

Detail: [[ownership-clarification]] for the clarification plan.

## How the Five Target Groups Interact

The five groups are not independent. Some of the dependencies:

| If you act on... | You also need... |
| --- | --- |
| Group 1 (delivery) | Group 5 (delivery tooling needs a decision owner) |
| Group 2 (backend) | Group 5 (backend convergence needs system-level ownership) |
| Group 3 (AWS accounts) | Group 5 (estate needs an owner before it can be consolidated) |
| Group 4 (legacy technologies) | Groups 2 and 3 (most legacy is anchored to backend systems or accounts) |
| Group 5 (ownership) | Groups 1-4 to be in scope, so ownership is being clarified for something concrete |

Group 5 is **the most cross-cutting**. It is also the most likely to fail by being done abstractly: clarifying ownership for "everything" tends to produce nothing. Ownership clarification works when it is **scoped to the systems being acted on** in the other four groups.

## Recommended First Pass

If Organization Germany wants a manageable first wave, start here:

1. **Inventory manual and duplicate delivery paths** (Group 1).
2. **Create owner and status mapping for AWS accounts** (Group 3 + Group 5).
3. **Identify the top backend overlap zones** (Group 2).
4. **Identify the top unowned or ambiguously owned systems** (Group 5).
5. **Classify the highest-cost legacy technologies into retire, contain, or tolerate** (Group 4).

Each of these is an **inventory or mapping** exercise. That is intentional. The first wave is mostly about making the estate visible enough to act on. The action waves follow in [[prioritization]].

## Anti-Patterns

* **Treating "first targets" as a wishlist.** Each group is here for a reason; the selection logic is explicit. Adding a sixth group without re-running the selection logic dilutes the focus.
* **Starting with Group 4 in isolation.** Retiring legacy technology before the systems that depend on it have new homes (Group 2) produces a stalled retirement.
* **Confusing the inventory work with the action work.** The first pass is mostly inventories. Inventories are necessary, not sufficient.
* **Re-debating the target groups every quarter.** The groups are stable; what changes is the specific candidates within them. See [[prioritization]].

## Scope and Revisiting

Revisit the first targets when:

* The inventories from the first pass show that one of the groups is much smaller (or much larger) than the diagnosis suggested.
* A new fragmentation hotspot emerges that does not fit any of the five groups.
* One of the groups is materially complete - in which case it leaves the "first targets" list and is replaced.

The five groups are not permanent. They are the **first wave**.
