> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Operate the strategy lightly, explicitly, and close to real decisions. A named strategy owner maintains coherence. Domain and platform owners apply the strategy in their area. Teams work within defaults unless an exception is approved. A lightweight strategy review forum handles decisions with strategic impact. Exceptions and legacy stay visible through explicit registers. Blueprints and standards turn policy into practical defaults. Monthly reviews handle live decisions; quarterly reviews check whether the strategy still fits.

## Decision

The [[policy]] only matters if it is **applied**. This document defines the lightest operating model that makes that possible.

The operating principle is:

> Keep the strategy lightweight, explicit, and tied to real decisions.

Everything below is designed to satisfy that sentence. If an element of the operating model becomes heavyweight, ceremonial, or disconnected from real decisions, it is failing.

## How to Use This Operating Model

Use this record whenever strategy work risks becoming either too abstract or too bureaucratic. It should answer:

* Who owns the strategy narrative and review cadence?
* Where does a team bring a strategic exception?
* Which artifacts must exist before a decision can be considered governed?
* How are legacy and exceptions kept visible after the meeting ends?
* How does a team know whether a decision is local, blueprint-level, or strategy-level?

The operating model is intentionally small. If it starts producing meetings that do not change decisions, reduce it. If strategic decisions happen outside it and leave no record, strengthen it.

## What This Should Help You Do

This operating model should help a reader:

* Explain who owns strategy coherence, domain application, and team execution.
* Decide when a choice needs the strategy review forum, an exception, or only a local team decision.
* Understand why registers, blueprints, and review cadence are part of strategy execution.
* Recognise when governance is becoming either too weak to matter or too heavy to use.

Common misreadings to avoid:

| Misreading | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| "The forum owns the decisions." | Teams and owners still own decisions; the forum keeps strategic impact visible. |
| "Registers are administration." | Registers are how exceptions and legacy stop disappearing from view. |
| "Quarterly review means quarterly action." | Action happens continuously; quarterly review checks policy fit. |

## Ownership

The operating model has three roles. Each is named, not implicit.

### Strategy Owner

A single named strategy owner:

* Maintains the overall strategy narrative ([[strategy]]).
* Keeps [[policy]] aligned with the current business context.
* Coordinates updates across [[diagnosis]], [[implications]], [[policy]], [[operate]], and [[roadmap]].
* Escalates unresolved conflicts when decisions cut across teams or domains.

The strategy owner is not an architecture committee. The strategy owner is **the person who makes sure the strategy stays coherent and visible**.

### Domain and Platform Owners

Each major domain (e.g. listing, search, CRM) and each major platform surface (e.g. delivery tooling, AWS account estate, messaging) has a named owner. Those owners:

* Apply the strategy within their scope.
* Identify where local reality conflicts with policy.
* Propose standards, blueprints, and legacy retirement plans.
* Maintain visibility on exceptions and active fragmentation within their area.

Domain and platform owners are the **operational surface** of the strategy. Most decisions happen at this level, not at the strategy-owner level.

### Delivery Teams

Delivery teams:

* Use the preferred defaults from the blueprints ([[backend-convergence]], [[delivery-path]], [[aws-accounts]], [[ownership-clarification]]).
* Avoid introducing new fragmentation without justification.
* Document where local needs cannot currently fit the defaults.
* Surface legacy and ownership risks early rather than normalising them.

Teams are not asked to memorise the policy. They are asked to **start from the defaults** and to flag where the defaults do not fit.

## Core Mechanisms

The operating model uses five mechanisms. None of them is optional; all of them are deliberately lightweight.

### 1. Strategy Review Forum

A small, regular forum where decisions with strategic impact are reviewed.

Use it to:

* Review decisions that may **increase fragmentation**.
* Evaluate **exceptions** to architectural or delivery defaults.
* Review **cross-team simplification opportunities**.
* Resolve decisions that affect **ownership boundaries or shared platforms**.

What it is not:

* Not a general architecture committee.
* Not a gate for every change.
* Not a substitute for domain or platform ownership.

The test for whether something belongs in the review forum is the [[policy]]: does the proposal increase fragmentation, bypass a default, or change ownership of a shared platform? If yes, bring it. If no, do not.

### 2. Exception Process

Exceptions are **allowed**. They must be **explicit and temporary by default**.

Record for each exception:

| Field | What it captures |
| --- | --- |
| Policy / default being bypassed | Which decision rule or blueprint does not fit. |
| Reason the default does not fit | The concrete business or technical mismatch. |
| Justifying business or technical need | What forces the divergence. |
| Owner | Who is accountable for the exception. |
| Expected duration | When the exception is reviewed or expires. |
| Follow-up work | Containment, retirement, or migration triggered by the exception. |

**Goal:** stop hidden divergence from becoming permanent fragmentation.

**Non-goal:** eliminate all exceptions. A working exception process is healthier than a policy that nobody can deviate from.

### 3. Required Artifacts

The operating model is supported by a small, maintained set of artifacts:

| Artifact | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Diagnosis document | The current diagnosis. ([[diagnosis]]) |
| Strategic implications | The implications the policy must respect. ([[implications]]) |
| Policy document | The nine core policies. ([[policy]]) |
| Operating model | This document. |
| Roadmap | Phased transition. ([[roadmap]]) |
| Architecture and platform blueprints | Per-area defaults. ([[backend-convergence]], [[delivery-path]], [[aws-accounts]], [[ownership-clarification]]) |
| Technology standards or technology radar | The approved set of technologies. |
| Exception register | All approved exceptions, with owner and expiry. |
| Legacy register | All legacy items, with status: retire / consolidate / contain / tolerate. |

Keep these **concise** and **maintained**. A maintained two-page artifact beats a thorough thirty-page artifact that has fallen out of date.

### 4. Blueprints and Standards

Blueprints turn policy into practical defaults:

* Preferred backend patterns. ([[backend-convergence]])
* Preferred delivery path from code to production. ([[delivery-path]])
* Account and infrastructure usage rules. ([[aws-accounts]])
* Guidance for shared services and ownership boundaries. ([[ownership-clarification]])
* Legacy containment and retirement patterns.

Blueprints are the form the policy takes when teams meet it in their normal work. **Most engineers should never need to read [[policy]] directly** - they should be working from the blueprints.

### 5. Legacy and Exception Registers

These two registers are the operating model's memory.

**Legacy register** — for each legacy item, track:

* What it is.
* Where it is used.
* Who owns it.
* Why it still exists.
* Current strategy: **retire**, **consolidate**, **contain**, or **tolerate temporarily**.

**Exception register** — for each approved exception, track:

* Scope.
* Owner.
* Justification.
* Expiry or review date.
* Associated risks.

Both registers are **reviewed regularly** so temporary deviations do not silently become permanent fragmentation. The legacy register is the single most concrete artifact for tracking whether [[policy]] P4 (legacy retirement) is actually happening.

## Decision Flow

When a real decision arrives, work through it in this order:

1. **Start from the strategy [[policy]] and existing defaults.**
2. **Check whether an existing blueprint or standard** ([[backend-convergence]], [[delivery-path]], [[aws-accounts]], [[ownership-clarification]]) **already answers the question.**
3. **If yes, follow the default.**
4. **If no**, assess the option using the five decision rules in [[policy]].
5. **If the proposal adds fragmentation or bypasses defaults**, raise it through the strategy review forum.
6. **Record approved exceptions explicitly** in the exception register.
7. **Convert repeated decisions** into updated standards or blueprints. (Repetition is the signal that a default should change.)

This is the loop the operating model is shaped around. Most decisions stop at step 3.

## Review Cadence

### Monthly

* Review major **exceptions**.
* Review **proposed decisions with strategic impact**.
* Review **newly identified fragmentation or ownership issues**.

### Quarterly

* Review whether **policy still matches Germany's business reality**.
* Review **progress on simplification, delivery-path convergence, and legacy retirement**.
* Update **standards, blueprints, and roadmap priorities**.

### On Major Change

Revisit the strategy when one of the following changes materially:

* Business scope.
* Organisation structure.
* Platform ownership model.
* Architecture direction.
* Major delivery tooling decisions taken centrally elsewhere in AVIV Group.

## How Teams Should Use the Strategy

Use the strategy when:

* Planning major initiatives.
* Proposing new services, accounts, or platforms.
* Selecting delivery tooling.
* Deciding whether to modernise, contain, or retire legacy.
* Resolving unclear ownership or boundary questions.

Use it **before divergence is created**, not after.

A working pattern is: when a team is about to make a decision that may bypass a default, the conversation starts with "let's check the blueprint" rather than with "let's propose an exception". The exception process is the last step, not the first.

## How the Strategy Should Be Evaluated

Check, on the quarterly cycle:

* Is the **number of active patterns** decreasing or still growing?
* Are teams **converging on clearer defaults**?
* Is the **delivery path** becoming simpler and more consistent?
* Is **ownership** becoming easier to understand?
* Is **legacy being retired** or merely surrounded by newer layers?
* Are **delivery speed and operational stability** improving?

These are the same five success currencies that [[policy]] P9 commits to. If they are not moving, the strategy is not working - regardless of how many new initiatives have started.

## What This Operating Model Deliberately Avoids

| Avoided | Why |
| --- | --- |
| Architecture committees | They tend to gate everything and decide nothing. The review forum reviews; it does not approve every change. |
| Centralised decision-making for low-impact choices | Defeats the purpose of strong defaults and domain ownership. |
| Heavyweight RFC processes for every change | The default path should be "follow the blueprint", not "write a doc". |
| Permanent exceptions without review | The whole point of the exception register is to make them temporary by design. |
| Updating policy faster than operating artifacts | Policy that is not reflected in blueprints, registers, and reviews is policy that exists only on paper. |

## Anti-Patterns

* **Treating the strategy as optional reading** rather than a decision framework. If teams cannot tell which policy applies to a current decision, the operating model is not visible enough.
* **Allowing hidden exceptions** outside the review process. Hidden exceptions defeat P8.
* **Creating too many forums or approvals** for low-impact decisions. The operating model is supposed to be lighter than what it replaces.
* **Producing standards that no team can realistically apply.** A blueprint that no team uses is fiction.
* **Updating the policy frequently** without updating the operational artifacts. The artifacts are how the policy reaches engineers.
* **Using the strategy to justify local preference** rather than organisation-wide simplification. The decision rules in [[policy]] are not a customisable menu.

## Minimum Viable Operating State

The minimum to consider the operating model "in place":

* A named strategy owner exists.
* Each major domain and platform surface has a named owner.
* The strategy review forum meets on a regular cadence.
* The exception register exists and is being maintained.
* The legacy register exists and is being maintained.
* At least one blueprint per major area ([[backend-convergence]], [[delivery-path]], [[aws-accounts]], [[ownership-clarification]]) is published.
* The quarterly review has happened at least once.

If any of those is missing, that is the next operating-model gap to close.

## Scope and Revisiting

Revisit the operating model when:

* The strategy review forum is consistently used for the wrong kind of decisions.
* The exception register is empty for long stretches (suggesting hidden divergence) **or** is overflowing (suggesting the defaults are wrong).
* Teams are bypassing the operating model rather than working with it.
* Quarterly reviews stop producing changes to blueprints, registers, or roadmap.

In each case, the fix is usually to make the operating model **lighter** or **more visible**, not to add more process.
