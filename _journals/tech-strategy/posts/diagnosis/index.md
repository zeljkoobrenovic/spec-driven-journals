> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Diagnosis**: Organization Germany is carrying engineering complexity that no longer matches the business it actually runs. The estate was shaped by White-Label platform ambitions layered on top of older legacy that was never fully removed. Germany now operates as a stable single-brand business, but the landscape is still optimised for broader reuse, abstraction, and distribution. The main problem is **fragmentation**, not lack of technical sophistication. Backend, developer experience, and the AWS account estate are the strongest concentration areas.

## Diagnosis

This diagnosis is the load-bearing input for the rest of the [[strategy]]. Everything that follows in [[policy]], [[roadmap]], and the blueprints depends on this being right.

The diagnosis is deliberately a description, not a prescription. It identifies the problem and the evidence behind it. The strategic response is in [[implications]] and [[policy]].

## How to Use This Diagnosis

Use this record to test whether later strategic choices are solving the right problem. A proposal should be challenged if it assumes:

* The main issue is lack of modern technology rather than fragmentation.
* Germany still needs White-Label-scale optionality by default.
* Legacy can remain active indefinitely because newer systems exist beside it.
* Ownership confusion is a people problem rather than an architecture and operating-model problem.
* Delivery speed can improve without reducing the number of active paths teams must understand.

The diagnosis is not a blame document. It is the shared description of the estate that makes simplification legitimate strategic work.

## What This Should Help You Do

This diagnosis should help a reader:

* Describe the three architectural eras that coexist in the estate.
* Explain why fragmentation is the central problem, not a shortage of technical sophistication.
* Connect delivery friction, unclear ownership, onboarding difficulty, and quality issues back to structural complexity.
* Challenge a proposed solution that does not address one of the diagnosed causes.

Common misreadings to avoid:

| Misreading | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| "Legacy means bad engineering." | Legacy is the accumulated result of previous business and platform choices; the issue is unmanaged coexistence. |
| "The White-Label era was wrong." | It matched a different strategic context; the current problem is that the estate still carries that optionality. |
| "Diagnosis is enough." | Diagnosis only explains the problem; policy and roadmap decide what to do. |

## Confirmed Context

* Organization Germany is expected to operate as a **stable single-brand organisation for the next 2-3 years**.
* The main business expectation is **faster delivery and stability**.
* The most visible current consequences are **confusion, unclear ownership, poor quality, and difficult onboarding**.
* The strongest mismatch is in the **backend** and in **developer experience**.
* The White-Label strategy aimed to build a shared platform for many brands and countries, but Germany's current business context is much narrower than that.

## Three Architectural Eras Coexist

The current landscape includes three architectural eras running in parallel, with no clear target state:

| Era | Shape | Where it shows up |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Monolithic | C# web application + SQL Server stored procedures + direct database access | Older core product surfaces, parts of CRM and listings |
| Event-driven services | Java microservices on self-hosted Kafka, RabbitMQ messaging | Mid-era backend services, integration substrate |
| Modern serverless | Serverless-based services + microfrontends, newer AWS patterns | Newer product surfaces, White-Label-era components |

Each era is internally coherent. The problem is that all three are **active at once**, often within the same business domain, and there is no explicit strategic statement about which is the target.

The diagnosis is not that any of these eras is wrong. The diagnosis is that **carrying all three indefinitely** is too expensive for Germany's actual scope.

## Active Legacy Technologies

In addition to the three eras above, the estate still includes a long tail of legacy technologies and tools that survived previous migrations without an explicit retirement decision:

* Self-hosted **Kafka** and **RabbitMQ** (messaging substrate)
* **SQL Server** and **Windows servers**
* **Java**, **ASPX**, **JSP** runtimes
* **GitLab**, **Jenkins**, **Team Foundation Server** (TFS) for delivery
* **Silverlight** frontend surfaces
* **Akamai** edge dependencies
* **Oracle**, **MySQL**, **Couchbase**, **ClickHouse**, **Solr** datastores in various states of use

This is not a vendor list; it is a load-bearing observation. **Every one of these still needs ownership, operations, and engineer attention**. The set is too wide for an organisation of Germany's current scope to support comfortably.

## Current Shape, In Numbers

The starting position - rounded for clarity:

* Roughly **200 AWS accounts** in total.
* **400+** GitHub repositories.
* **1,000+** GitLab repositories.
* **20+** VSTS/TFS repositories.
* Approximately **30 million lines of code** across the three source-control systems.
* Delivery toolchain fragmented across **GitHub + CircleCI**, **GitLab + GitLab pipelines**, **TFS + manual**, and **Bitbucket + Jenkins**.

The AWS account split is itself diagnostic:

* About **113 legacy AWS accounts**.
* Roughly **100 White-Label-era accounts**.
* A long tail of **transitional or unclear-purpose accounts**.

The original landscape documents explicitly note that each team had its own workspace with **three AWS accounts** (`dev`, `preview`, `live`), and that this became confusing as team names and responsibilities changed. The artefact of that design is now an estate that no one team can fully account for.

## Core Diagnosis

> Organization Germany has a mismatch between inherited complexity and actual business need.

The current estate was shaped by:

* **White-Label platform ambitions** designed for a broader scope than Germany now operates in.
* **Older legacy that was never fully removed** when newer eras were introduced.

Germany now operates in a much narrower reality, but still carries a landscape built for broader reuse, abstraction, and distribution.

This mismatch is strongest in three areas:

1. **Backend architecture** - three eras in parallel, no clear target.
2. **Developer experience** - fragmented delivery path from code to production.
3. **Infrastructure / account structure** - account estate sized for a multi-brand, multi-country platform.

The main issue is **fragmentation**, not lack of technical sophistication.

## Evidence Summary

The diagnosis is grounded in observable evidence from the existing landscape materials, not in opinion:

| Evidence | What it shows |
| --- | --- |
| Source code: 400+ GitHub + 1,000+ GitLab + 20+ TFS repos, ~30 MLOC | Code estate spans three generations of source-control tooling; no single system of record. |
| Cloud: ~113 legacy + ~100 WL accounts | Account estate is structurally two estates, not one. |
| Each team historically had 3 AWS accounts (`dev`/`preview`/`live`) | Account-as-workspace model now produces ownership ambiguity as teams change. |
| Delivery: GitHub+CircleCI, GitLab+GitLab pipelines, TFS+manual, Bitbucket+Jenkins, manual builds | No converged code-to-production path. |
| Technology surfaces: SQL Server, Oracle, MySQL, Couchbase, ClickHouse, Kafka, RabbitMQ, Windows EC2, JBoss, Solr, Java, ASPX, JSP, Vue, Jenkins, GitLab, TFS, Silverlight, Akamai | Non-golden-path technologies are not exceptional cases; they are routine. |
| AWS landscape diagrams visibly separate legacy and White-Label estates | Structural fragmentation is visible at the topology level, not only inside individual systems. |

The materials do not just show technical age. They show **structural fragmentation across code, cloud accounts, tooling, and operational boundaries**.

## Why Fragmentation Hurts More Than It Helps

Fragmentation is not free. It is paid for, continuously, in five currencies:

* **Delivery speed.** Every team's path from code to production looks different, so onboarding is per-team, tooling is per-team, and there is no shared muscle for shipping safely.
* **Operational stability.** Each tool, runtime, and account adds an independent failure mode. The more variety, the wider the surface area that has to be monitored, patched, and recovered.
* **Ownership clarity.** Systems that span eras tend to span teams. When a problem appears, it is not obvious who decides on the fix.
* **Onboarding.** A new engineer has to learn three backend styles, four delivery tool combinations, and a long tail of legacy surfaces before they can be productive. The cost compounds for every hire.
* **Quality.** Engineering effort spent compensating for the estate is effort not spent on product. Quality decays where attention is scarcest.

These five currencies are how the strategy [[policy]] later defines success.

## Why This Matters Now

The business expectation is **faster delivery and stability**.

The current estate works against both.

Without simplification and convergence, Organization Germany will continue to pay a tax in delivery speed, operational stability, ownership clarity, onboarding, and quality. The tax compounds: every new initiative built on top of the fragmented estate inherits the tax, and often adds to it.

The diagnosis is therefore not "the platform is bad". It is "the platform is bigger than the business now needs, and the cost of carrying that extra surface is now visibly larger than the value it returns".

## What the Diagnosis Does **Not** Say

| What the diagnosis says | What the diagnosis does **not** say |
| --- | --- |
| The estate is too fragmented for Germany's current scope. | The estate is broken or poorly engineered. |
| Three architectural eras coexist without a target. | One of the three eras is the right answer. |
| The White-Label strategy left Germany with surplus optionality. | The White-Label work was a mistake. |
| Legacy was never fully removed. | All legacy must be removed quickly. |
| Backend and developer experience are the strongest mismatches. | Other areas are fine. |
| Fragmentation is the main problem. | Sophistication is the problem. |

Each pair is the kind of distinction that will matter when applying the [[policy]] in real decisions.

## Boundaries and Assumptions

* The diagnosis is focused on **identifying the problem**, not prescribing the solution. The prescription lives in [[policy]] and the blueprints.
* The diagnosis **assumes** that Germany's single-brand scope remains stable during the strategy horizon. If that changes materially, revisit the strategy ([[strategy]] § Scope and Revisiting).
* The diagnosis treats **fragmentation and inherited complexity** as the primary strategic problem, with **backend** and **developer experience** as the most important concentration areas.
* The diagnosis does **not** yet rank individual systems, teams, or technologies by migration priority. That is the job of [[first-targets]] and [[prioritization]].
* The diagnosis does **not** define a target architecture, roadmap, or governance model. Those live in [[roadmap]] and [[operate]].
