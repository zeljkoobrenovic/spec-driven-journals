> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Make system, platform, and legacy ownership explicit. Each major system, platform surface, and legacy estate has one accountable owner. Where ownership is genuinely shared, separate operational owner from decision owner. First-wave hotspots: the legacy AWS estate, the delivery tooling estate, and shared legacy messaging and integration surfaces (Kafka, RabbitMQ). Every major system or estate must have a named owner; every shared platform surface must have a named decision owner; legacy without an owner is not acceptable.

## Decision

This document is the **ownership clarification plan**: the operational expression of [[policy]] P5 ("Ownership boundaries must become clear") and implication 6 in [[implications]] ("Ownership clarity must be designed together with technical simplification").

Ownership clarity is the **most cross-cutting** of the strategy concerns. It is also the most likely to fail by being done abstractly: clarifying ownership for "everything at once" tends to produce nothing. This plan therefore names specific first-wave hotspots and treats other ownership work as a continuous output of the [[operate]] mechanisms.

## Why This Matters

The [[diagnosis]] identifies unclear ownership as a direct contributor to:

* **Confusion** about decisions.
* **Poor quality** in shared systems.
* **Difficult onboarding** for new engineers.
* **Stalled simplification** when no one is accountable for retirement.

Unclear ownership is also a **prerequisite** for the deeper convergence work in later roadmap phases. [[roadmap]] Phase 2 → Phase 3 is the direct example: backend convergence under unclear accountability produces a louder version of the existing problem.

The "everyone depends on it, no one owns it" pattern is the version of unclear ownership that hurts the most. The legacy AWS estate, the delivery tooling estate, and the shared messaging substrate all currently fit this pattern.

## How to Use This Plan

Use this plan when a decision stalls because ownership is unclear. It should answer:

* Which system, platform surface, or legacy estate is being discussed?
* Who operates it today?
* Who is allowed to decide its future?
* Which teams depend on it?
* Is ownership unclear because responsibility is genuinely shared, or because no team has accepted it?
* What decision becomes possible once ownership is clarified?

The plan is not a reorganisation document. It is a practical mechanism for making technical responsibility explicit enough that simplification, retirement, and convergence can proceed.

## What This Should Help You Do

This plan should help a reader:

* Distinguish operational ownership from decision ownership.
* Identify ownership hotspots that block simplification.
* Explain why "shared ownership" needs explicit boundaries to be useful.
* Turn an unclear system, platform surface, or legacy estate into an owner-finding action.

Common misreadings to avoid:

| Misreading | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| "Ownership clarification is org design." | It is technical accountability for systems, platforms, and legacy surfaces. |
| "Shared ownership is always bad." | Shared ownership can be valid, but operational and decision roles must be explicit. |
| "Naming an owner solves the problem." | Ownership must include authority, capacity, and lifecycle responsibility. |

## Clarification Goal

Move toward:

* **One clear accountable owner** for each major system, platform surface, and legacy estate.
* **Explicit distinction** between operational owner and decision owner where the two are genuinely different.
* **Fewer shared-ownership areas.**
* **Fewer systems that everyone depends on but no one truly owns.**

## Operational vs Decision Ownership

The plan distinguishes two roles where the distinction matters:

| Role | What they do | When they are the same person | When they are different |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| **Operational owner** | Runs it day to day. On-call. Responds to incidents. Operates the day-to-day exception process. | Most product services. | Shared platforms where a central team operates and a domain team decides on evolution. |
| **Decision owner** | Decides on its evolution, retirement, exceptions, or strategic changes. | Most product services. | Shared platforms, legacy estates with operational caretakers but no strategic owner. |

For most product services, these are the same person or team. The distinction becomes important - and is most often where the strategy stalls - on shared platform surfaces and legacy estates.

## First-Wave Hotspots

The plan focuses on three named hotspots. These are the cases where the gap between current state and required state is largest, and where action is most realistic.

### 1. Legacy AWS Estate

**Why this is first.** The estate is fragmented across historical teams and eras, with no single clear owner.

**Goal:**

* **Identify accountable owner or owners** for the legacy AWS estate.
* **Separate operational responsibility from strategic decision ownership** if needed.

**Practical shape.** The legacy estate likely needs:

* One **decision owner** for the legacy estate as a whole - someone who can authorise consolidation, retirement, and exception decisions.
* **Operational owners** at the level of individual systems within the estate, mapped to the systems they actually operate today.

Connected to: [[aws-accounts]] for the consolidation plan that depends on this ownership being in place.

### 2. Delivery Tooling Estate

**Why this is first.** No single clear default owner exists across the whole code-to-production path today. The estate spans GitHub, CircleCI, GitLab, TFS, Jenkins, and manual paths.

**Goal:**

* **Establish who owns the preferred path from code to production.**
* **Make it clear who can approve, retire, or standardise** delivery paths.

**Practical shape.** The delivery tooling estate likely needs:

* One **decision owner** for the preferred path - the person who can approve standardisation decisions through [[operate]].
* **Operational owners** for each currently-active path while convergence is in progress.
* A **named retirement owner** for each path on the retirement list.

Connected to: [[delivery-path]] for the blueprint and convergence plan that depends on this ownership being in place.

### 3. Shared Legacy Messaging and Integration Surfaces

**Why this is first.** Self-hosted Kafka, RabbitMQ, and the related integration surfaces are the canonical "everyone depends on, no one owns" example. Leaving them in that state blocks both backend convergence and legacy retirement.

**Goal:**

* **Identify who owns Kafka, RabbitMQ, and the related integration decisions.**
* **Avoid leaving shared messaging surfaces in a permanent "everyone / no one" state.**

**Practical shape.** Shared messaging likely needs:

* One **decision owner** per messaging substrate (Kafka, RabbitMQ).
* An **explicit decision** about each substrate: contain, simplify, or retire (per [[policy]] P4).
* **Containment rules** that prevent further sprawl while the decision is pending.

Connected to: [[backend-convergence]] for the convergence plan that needs the messaging shape clarified.

## Minimum Rules

| Rule | Why |
| --- | --- |
| Every major system or estate must have a **named owner**. | Defeats P5 of [[policy]] otherwise. |
| Every shared platform surface must have a **named decision owner**. | "Everyone / no one" is the failure mode this plan exists to prevent. |
| If ownership is **shared**, the split must be **explicit**. | Implicit shared ownership is invisible shared ownership. |
| **Legacy without owner** is not acceptable. | Unowned legacy is the diagnosis pattern restated as a current operating principle. |

These four rules are the bare minimum the operating model should enforce through the strategy review forum.

## Practical Questions to Ask of Any System

When clarifying ownership for a specific system, estate, or platform:

* **Who operates it day to day?**
* **Who decides on its evolution or retirement?**
* **Who approves exceptions related to it?**
* **Who is accountable when simplification work stalls?**

The fourth question is the most often skipped. It is also the most diagnostic: if no one is accountable when simplification stalls, simplification will stall.

The starting view of the three named hotspots:

| System / Platform / Estate | Current Owner | Ownership Issue | Candidate Action |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |

The inventory is meant to be **maintained**, not snapshotted once. As the operating model produces clearer ownership in these three areas, new ownership hotspots will surface; they go on the same inventory.

## Immediate Actions

The concrete first-wave work for the ownership plan:

1. **Review the ownership inventory** above with domain and platform owners.
2. **Mark every hotspot** as: clear owner / unclear owner / shared owner / unowned.
3. **For each unclear or shared area**, define:
   * Operational owner.
   * Decision owner.
4. **Escalate unresolved ownership gaps** through the strategy review forum ([[operate]]).

The output of these four steps is the first version of the **ownership clarification wave plan** that [[operate]] reviews on the monthly cadence.

## Success Criteria

The ownership clarification plan is working when:

* **Fewer systems** have shared or unclear ownership.
* **Clearer accountability** for simplification and retirement work.
* **Easier decision-making** for delivery, platform, and legacy changes.
* Stalled simplification work in any of the first-wave hotspots can be **traced to a named owner** (or to the absence of one, which is then a tractable problem).

These are restatements of [[policy]] P9 in ownership-specific form.

## What This Plan Deliberately Does Not Do

| Not specified | Why |
| --- | --- |
| A specific organisational structure | Ownership is about accountability, not org chart. |
| Resolving every shared-ownership case | The plan focuses on hotspots first; the long tail is continuous work in [[operate]]. |
| Specific named individuals | This is a plan; the names are assigned through [[operate]]. |
| A specific decision-rights framework (RACI, etc.) | The minimum rules above are the framework. Layering RACI on top is optional. |
| Reorganising teams | Team reorganisation is downstream of, not a substitute for, named ownership. |

## Anti-Patterns

* **"This is owned by the platform team."** "The platform team" is not a named decision owner. Name the person or team-of-one who can approve exceptions.
* **"It's a shared responsibility."** Shared without an explicit split is unowned in practice.
* **"We'll assign an owner once we know the system better."** Unowned systems do not get to know themselves; named owners are how systems become known.
* **"Operational and decision ownership are obviously the same."** Often they are, but assuming it for shared platforms is exactly how the current diagnosis was produced.
* **"Ownership is an org problem, not a tech problem."** [[implications]] 6 says explicitly: ownership clarity is part of the technical work, not separate from it. Refactors that leave ownership unclear are incomplete.

## Scope and Revisiting

Revisit the plan when:

* The first wave produces owners for the three hotspots - in which case the next-wave hotspots replace them.
* A new ownership hotspot emerges that is more severe than the three currently named.
* Repeated escalations to the strategy review forum show that the minimum rules above are not enforceable in the current organisational shape.

In the absence of those triggers, the plan should be **stable**. The hotspots in it are not.
