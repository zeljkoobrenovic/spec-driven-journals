> **Status**: DRAFT
>
> **Decision**: Optimise for Germany-scale needs by simplifying, converging, reducing variance, clarifying ownership, and retiring or containing legacy. Nine core policies cover direction, guidance, approval, and measurement. Five decision rules apply at the point of choice: prefer options that reduce fragmentation, fit Germany's actual scope, simplify delivery, clarify ownership, and create a path to retire legacy. Reject options that add durable complexity without clear Germany-specific value.

## Decision

This is the policy that operationalises the [[strategy]]. It is the part of the strategy that most directly answers "what do I do when I have a real choice in front of me?".

The policy has three components:

1. **Nine core policies** that state the direction.
2. **Five decision rules** that apply at the point of choice.
3. **A list of things to refuse**, so the policy can be applied negatively when no positive option fits.

The policy is grounded in [[diagnosis]] and [[implications]]. Each policy connects to specific diagnosis themes; this is not a wishlist.

## How to Apply This Policy

Apply the policy at the moment a team has a real choice: new service shape, migration path, CI/CD tool, AWS account boundary, ownership model, or exception. The policy should turn a vague strategic preference into a concrete yes/no/why conversation.

Use the five decision rules in order:

1. Does the option reduce fragmentation?
2. Does it fit Germany's actual scope?
3. Does it simplify delivery?
4. Does it clarify ownership?
5. Does it create a path to retire or contain legacy?

If an option fails several rules, the burden of proof is on the proposer. If an option passes the rules but has meaningful local cost, that cost should be documented rather than used as a reason to preserve the fragmented default.

## What This Should Help You Do

This policy should help a reader:

* Apply the nine policies to a concrete technical decision.
* Use the five decision rules without turning them into a bureaucratic checklist.
* Explain why some proposals should be refused even when they are locally attractive.
* Identify which blueprint or operating mechanism should handle an exception.

Common misreadings to avoid:

| Misreading | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| "Policy means central approval for everything." | Most local decisions stay local; policy constrains decisions with strategic impact. |
| "Exceptions are forbidden." | Exceptions are allowed when explicit, owned, and reviewed. |
| "Reducing variance means no innovation." | Innovation is still possible, but new durable fragmentation must earn its cost. |

## Policy Summary

Optimise for Germany-scale needs by:

* Simplifying.
* Converging.
* Reducing variance.
* Clarifying ownership.
* Retiring or containing legacy.

## The Nine Core Policies

### P1. Simplification and Convergence Are Primary Objectives

**Type:** Direction.

**Addresses:** fragmentation, excessive complexity, mismatch between current estate and Germany's actual scope.

**Intent.** Reduce unnecessary complexity across architecture, tooling, infrastructure, and operating model.

**Why.** The [[diagnosis]] shows that Germany is carrying more complexity than its current business scope justifies. The main problem is fragmentation, not lack of sophistication.

**What this means:**

* Simplification work is **strategic** work, not cleanup.
* Plans should prefer fewer active patterns over more optionality.
* Modernisation should remove active complexity, not add parallel layers.
* Convergence is judged by whether teams can build, operate, and change systems more easily.

**Allowed:**

* Simplifying architecture even when it is less theoretically flexible.
* Consolidating tooling, services, or infrastructure if it reduces operational burden.
* Preferring proven, understandable patterns over more advanced but less necessary ones.

**Not allowed by default:**

* Adding new patterns because they are more modern.
* Preserving historical complexity without a current business reason.
* Treating simplification as deferrable background work forever.

**Decision test.** If a change does not reduce fragmentation, improve clarity, or create a path to remove old complexity, it should be challenged.

### P2. Backend Convergence Is the Main Technical Direction

**Type:** Direction.

**Addresses:** three backend eras in parallel, unclear target state, backend complexity.

**Intent.** Reduce the number of backend architectural styles in active use.

**Why.** Germany currently operates monolithic, event-driven microservice, and newer serverless-era patterns in parallel. That weakens clarity and increases cognitive load.

**What this means:**

* New backend work should align with a **narrower target direction**.
* Backend design should optimise for **understandability, ownership, and operability**.
* The backend default should become **clearer over time**, not less clear.

**Allowed:**

* Evolving legacy systems toward the target direction incrementally.
* Using a conventional microservice model where service boundaries are clear and justified.
* Keeping selected legacy systems when containment is a better choice than forced migration.

**Not allowed by default:**

* Introducing a fourth backend style.
* Mixing multiple backend paradigms in one area without a strong reason.
* Using serverless-first architecture as the default for new product backend development.

**Decision test.** Does this backend decision reduce overlap between current paradigms, or does it add another path?

The full backend blueprint and convergence plan live in [[backend-convergence]].

### P3. Delivery-Path Simplification Is Part of the Core Strategy

**Type:** Direction.

**Addresses:** fragmented delivery tooling, slower delivery, weaker stability.

**Intent.** Make the path from code to production simpler, more consistent, and easier to operate.

**Why.** The [[diagnosis]] shows fragmented delivery across GitHub, CircleCI, GitLab, TFS, Jenkins, and manual paths. This directly hurts speed and stability.

**What this means:**

* CI/CD is **part of engineering strategy**, not just tooling hygiene.
* The number of build and deployment paths should **shrink over time**.
* Manual builds and manual deployments should be reduced deliberately.

**Allowed:**

* Standardising delivery tooling even if it requires migration effort.
* Retiring low-value custom pipelines.
* Defining a preferred path and requiring teams to justify exceptions.

**Not allowed by default:**

* Adding new delivery tooling without strategic reason.
* Treating manual production changes as a normal operating model.
* Allowing each team to keep its own toolchain indefinitely without challenge.

**Decision test.** Will this make the code-to-production path easier to understand, support, and operate across teams?

The full delivery blueprint and convergence plan live in [[delivery-path]].

### P4. Legacy Retirement Is Mandatory

**Type:** Direction.

**Addresses:** unresolved legacy, incomplete migrations, excessive technology diversity.

**Intent.** Ensure modernisation actually reduces the active legacy footprint.

**Why.** Germany's current estate is the result of repeated partial migrations where new layers were added but old ones remained.

**What this means:**

* Every major modernisation effort **must state what old complexity it removes**.
* Legacy systems need **explicit status**: retire, consolidate, contain, or tolerate temporarily.
* Legacy **without an owner or declared status** is not acceptable.

**Allowed:**

* Temporary coexistence when migration risk requires it.
* Containment strategies when immediate retirement is unrealistic.
* Prioritising the most painful legacy first rather than trying to remove everything at once.

**Not allowed by default:**

* Adding new platforms while leaving old ones active without a retirement or containment plan.
* Calling a migration complete while both old and new remain equally active.
* Tolerating legacy because ownership is unclear.

**Decision test.** What old complexity will this change remove, contain, or make easier to retire?

### P5. Ownership Boundaries Must Become Clear

**Type:** Direction.

**Addresses:** unclear ownership, fragmented responsibilities, difficult onboarding.

**Intent.** Make responsibility for systems, platforms, and legacy explicit and operationally useful.

**Why.** The [[diagnosis]] identifies unclear ownership as a direct contributor to confusion, poor quality, and difficult onboarding.

**What this means:**

* Major systems and platform surfaces need **accountable owners**.
* Ownership should align with system boundaries and operating responsibilities.
* **Shared ownership should be minimised.**

**Allowed:**

* Redrawing boundaries to improve accountability.
* Consolidating services or responsibilities where fragmented ownership creates cost.
* Assigning explicit owners to legacy estates even when those systems are temporary.

**Not allowed by default:**

* Shared components with no clear decision-maker.
* Unowned legacy.
* Organisational structures that make system responsibility impossible to understand.

**Decision test.** After this change, will it be clearer who owns the system, who operates it, and who decides on its evolution?

The full ownership clarification plan lives in [[ownership-clarification]].

### P6. The Number of Supported Technologies and Toolchains Must Decrease Over Time

**Type:** Direction.

**Addresses:** stack diversity, cognitive load, quality risk.

**Intent.** Shrink the active technology footprint to reduce cognitive load and quality risk.

**Why.** Germany currently supports too many technologies, runtimes, tools, and platforms across multiple generations.

**What this means:**

* The strategic set of technologies should become **smaller**.
* **Tolerated legacy must not be confused with strategic default.**
* Introducing a new technology should be **harder** than staying within an approved set.

**Allowed:**

* Keeping a technology temporarily when migration cost is high.
* Selecting from an existing strategic set.
* Retiring a technology even when that means reducing optionality.

**Not allowed by default:**

* Adding new languages, databases, messaging systems, or CI/CD tools without strong justification.
* Letting every team optimise for local preference.
* Keeping obsolete tools active forever because replacement is inconvenient.

**Decision test.** Does this reduce the supported footprint, preserve it deliberately, or expand it?

### P7. Strong Defaults Are Preferred Over Broad Optionality

**Type:** Guidance.

**Addresses:** too many parallel choices, local variation, lack of clarity.

**Intent.** Reduce ambiguity in everyday engineering decisions.

**Why.** The estate already has too many parallel choices. Optionality now creates more cost than value.

**What this means:**

* Blueprints and standards should define **default choices**.
* Teams should start from defaults, not from a blank page.
* Deviations should be **visible and deliberate**.

**Allowed:**

* Enforcing standard paths where consistency matters.
* Using guidance where the decision is still somewhat ambiguous.
* Creating a small number of explicit exceptions.

**Not allowed by default:**

* Unlimited local choice in architecture, tooling, or infrastructure.
* Vague defaults that require each team to reinterpret them.
* Passive acceptance of drift from the standard path.

**Decision test.** Will this make future decisions easier and more consistent, or increase local variation?

### P8. New Fragmentation Requires Explicit Justification

**Type:** Approval.

**Addresses:** account sprawl, platform sprawl, new architectural variance.

**Intent.** Put a high barrier in front of changes that increase structural complexity.

**Why.** Without this control, the current problem will continue reproducing itself.

**What this means:**

* Adding new accounts, platforms, pipelines, or architecture styles **needs review**.
* Justification must be **explicit, not assumed**.
* Exceptions should be **tracked**.

**Allowed:**

* Approved exceptions for concrete business or technical reasons.
* Targeted divergence when existing defaults clearly do not fit.
* Temporary local solutions with explicit expiry or review.

**Not allowed by default:**

* Silent creation of new fragmentation.
* Assuming a local need automatically justifies a new platform or pattern.
* Keeping approved exceptions invisible.

**Decision test.** Is this creating a new permanent source of fragmentation, or is there a strong reason why the default cannot work?

### P9. Success Is Measured by Delivery Speed, Stability, Quality, Onboarding, and Ownership Clarity

**Type:** Direction.

**Addresses:** the outcomes the strategy is ultimately accountable for.

**Intent.** Keep the strategy tied to operational and business outcomes.

**Why.** The strategy is not about architectural elegance. It is about making the engineering system work better for Germany.

**What this means:**

* Technical choices should be evaluated by **outcome**, not only by design purity.
* Metrics and review should reflect **delivery and operational performance**.
* Improvements that do not change outcomes should be **questioned**.

**Allowed:**

* Choosing a less novel solution if it improves delivery and stability.
* Rejecting technically interesting proposals that do not improve outcomes.
* Measuring success with a mix of qualitative and quantitative signals.

**Not allowed by default:**

* Treating modernity as success.
* Calling a strategy effective if engineers still cannot deliver faster or operate more safely.
* Separating technology choices from ownership, onboarding, and quality consequences.

**Decision test.** If this policy is followed consistently, will teams deliver faster, operate more safely, and work with clearer ownership?

## The Five Decision Rules

When choosing between options at any level - tool, platform, architecture, account, ownership - apply these rules in order. The earlier rules outrank the later ones when they conflict.

| Rule | Prefer the option that... |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Reduces fragmentation. |
| 2 | Fits Germany's actual business scope. |
| 3 | Simplifies the delivery path. |
| 4 | Clarifies ownership. |
| 5 | Creates a path to retire legacy. |

And the negative rule:

* **Reject** options that add durable complexity without clear Germany-specific value.

These five rules are deliberately simple. They are the form the policy takes when there is no time to read the full document.

## What This Means in Practice (Things to Refuse)

The policy is most useful when applied negatively. Do not:

* Add abstraction, reuse layers, or platform complexity without a concrete Germany need.
* Preserve multiple backend paradigms in parallel by default.
* Allow the active technology and tooling set to grow without strong reason.
* Treat migrations as complete if the old layer remains active without containment or retirement.
* Accept manual delivery paths as a normal steady state.
* Allow shared ownership and unclear boundaries to persist without challenge.
* Create new accounts, platforms, pipelines, messaging systems, or architecture styles by default.

If a proposal cannot be reconciled with at least one of the five decision rules above, and falls into one of these "do not" categories, the answer is **no** unless an explicit exception is approved through the [[operate]] process.

## Enforceability Assumptions

These policies assume:

* Leadership is willing to prioritise simplification alongside delivery.
* Architecture and platform decisions with strategic impact can be reviewed centrally.
* Teams can be required to justify new fragmentation.
* Some capacity can be allocated to simplification, convergence, and retirement work.

If these assumptions do not hold, the operating model ([[operate]]) or the diagnosis must be adjusted. The policy itself becomes ceremonial otherwise.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Quoting policies in isolation to win arguments.** The decision rules apply *together*, in order, with the diagnosis as backdrop. P7 ("strong defaults") cited alone can be twisted into "no exceptions ever"; that is not what the policy says.
* **Treating the policy as a checklist.** The policy is a decision aid. A proposal that ticks every box but does not actually reduce fragmentation is still failing the policy.
* **Using the policy to suppress all autonomy.** Strong defaults plus a working exception process is the design. Defaults with no exception path is something else.
* **Updating the policy to match a desired outcome.** If the policy needs to change, the [[diagnosis]] or [[implications]] should change first. The policy is downstream of those.

## Scope and Revisiting

Revisit policy in two cases:

1. **Quarterly**, through the operating mechanisms in [[operate]], to check whether each policy still matches Germany's business reality and whether the diagnosis is still load-bearing.
2. **On major change** to business scope, organisation structure, platform ownership model, or central AVIV Group direction.

The policy is intended to be **stable**. Frequent rewriting suggests the diagnosis is unsettled rather than the policy being wrong.
