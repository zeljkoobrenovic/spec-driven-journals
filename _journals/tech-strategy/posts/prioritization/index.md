Sum the five dimensions for the priority score. Guardrail: do not prioritise by ease alone. Apply the method to the candidates from [[first-targets]]. ## Decision

This post is the **method + first instance** of how candidates become waves of execution. It takes the target groups from [[first-targets]], scores specific candidates, and produces an initial wave plan that can be adopted by the [[operate]] review forum.

The method is deliberately simple. Optimising the scoring is a way to avoid making the call; the scoring is a decision aid, not a decision.

## How to Use This Scoring

Use the score to make tradeoffs explicit, not to automate strategy. The review forum should use the score to ask:

* Which dimension is driving the score?
* Which score is evidence-backed, and which is an estimate?
* Is the candidate actionable now, or does it need ownership and inventory work first?
* Are we choosing a real simplification candidate, or an easy local cleanup task?
* What would have to change for this item to move waves?

The output of scoring is a wave proposal with reasoning. It is not a permanent ranking and it is not a substitute for ownership.

## What This Should Help You Do

This record should help a reader:

* Score simplification candidates using the five dimensions.
* Explain why ease of action is useful but dangerous when used alone.
* Distinguish Wave 1, Wave 2, Investigation Queue, Contain, and Defer.
* Challenge a score by asking for evidence rather than arguing from preference.

Common misreadings to avoid:

| Misreading | Correction |
| --- | --- |
| "Highest score always goes first." | Dependencies and ambiguity still matter. |
| "The model is objective." | The model structures judgement; it does not replace judgement. |
| "Investigation means low priority." | Investigation often means high impact but not yet actionable. |

## Scoring Dimensions

Score each candidate from `1` to `5` on five dimensions.

### 1. Delivery Friction

* `1` — low impact on delivery.
* `5` — major blocker to delivery speed.

### 2. Stability Risk

* `1` — low stability concern.
* `5` — major reliability or operational risk.

### 3. Ownership Confusion

* `1` — ownership already clear.
* `5` — ownership repeatedly unclear or disputed.

### 4. Fragmentation Impact

* `1` — isolated issue.
* `5` — high-fragmentation hotspot affecting multiple teams or systems.

### 5. Ease Of Action

* `1` — very hard to act on now.
* `5` — realistic to contain, simplify, or retire soon.

## Priority Score

> `Priority Score = Delivery Friction + Stability Risk + Ownership Confusion + Fragmentation Impact + Ease Of Action`

Higher score = stronger candidate for early action. Range: 5-25.

## Decision Heuristic

| Profile | Action |
| --- | --- |
| High score + low ambiguity | Early wave candidate. |
| High score + high ambiguity | Investigate first. |
| Low score + high effort | Defer. |
| High score from "ease" alone, low everywhere else | Reconsider - probably a small win wearing a strategic costume. |

## Guardrail

**Do not prioritise only by ease of action.**

The goal of the strategy is not quick wins. The goal is to remove **meaningful sources of friction**. Candidates that score `5` on ease but `1`/`2` on the other four are not what this method is meant to surface.

The opposite failure mode also exists: candidates that score `5` on everything except ease often end up doing nothing. Those belong in the **Investigation Queue**, not in an action wave.

### Notes on backend candidates

* **Core classifieds / listing flow** is the strongest backend convergence candidate because it is business-central and appears to span monolith, service, and newer distributed shapes at the same time. It is **Investigate** in this round because it currently has low ease of action - it needs overlap mapping, ownership clarity, and a target backend direction first.
* **Messaging and shared integration estate** scores high because it likely drives complexity across multiple domains, but it needs dependency mapping before action.
* **Search and discovery** and **User / account / CRM** are important overlap areas, but they stay second-tier until ownership and system boundaries are clearer.
* **Data / analytics / DWH** matters, but it is more likely to be a slower and more dependency-heavy convergence area than an early delivery-focused win.

The recommendation values used in the table:

* **Wave 1** — high-friction, high-clarity candidates with realistic ease of action.
* **Wave 2** — high-impact candidates that depend on Wave 1 outputs.
* **Investigate** — high-impact candidates with low ease of action; need mapping first.
* **Contain** — items that are not going to be retired in the strategy horizon but must stop expanding.
* **Defer** — items below the threshold for either current wave.

## How Waves Become Work

Once a Wave 1 candidate is approved by the [[operate]] review forum:

1. The candidate gets a **named owner**.
2. The candidate gets a **decision date** (not necessarily a delivery date) - when the next checkpoint is.
3. Status transitions from **Proposed** → **In Progress** → **Done** or **Replaced** in the wave plan.
4. Repeated scoring of similar candidates produces updates to the **blueprints** and the **legacy register** (in [[operate]]).

A candidate that lingers in **Proposed** for two quarters without becoming **In Progress** is a signal that either the diagnosis is wrong about that candidate, or the operating model is failing to assign owners.

## Anti-Patterns

* **Optimising the scoring instead of acting on it.** The method is intentionally simple. Long debates about whether a score should be `3` or `4` are usually displacement activity.
* **Re-scoring everything quarterly to look productive.** Most items do not change category. Re-score on signal, not on cadence.
* **Prioritising by ease alone.** The guardrail is explicit; ignoring it produces a backlog of trivial wins that does not move the diagnosis.
* **Promoting Investigation Queue items to Wave 2 to make the plan look bigger.** Items in the queue are there because they have a known unknown. Promoting them before that unknown is resolved is how Wave 2 stalls.
* **Treating Wave 1 as the whole strategy.** Wave 1 is the **first** wave, not the **only** wave. The harder backend and infrastructure work in Wave 2 is the bulk of the strategy's value.

## Scope and Revisiting

Revisit the prioritisation at the quarterly review in [[operate]]:

* **Update scores** for candidates whose key unknowns have been resolved.
* **Move items** between Investigation Queue and Wave 2 as conditions change.
* **Refresh Wave 1** once enough of its candidates are in progress or done.
* **Challenge candidates** that have been in Proposed for more than two quarters.

The scoring method itself is stable. The candidates and their scores are the part that moves.
