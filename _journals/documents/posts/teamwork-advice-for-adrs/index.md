> Three lessons from rolling out decision records with many teams: lead with the why, prefer the word \"decisions\" to \"ADRs\", and treat ADRs as living documents in practice.

If you're considering decision records with your team, here are three lessons we've picked up from working with many of them.

## Lead with the why, not the what

You have an opportunity to lead by *talking together about why* rather than mandating *what*. Decision records are a way for teams to think more clearly and communicate better. They are worthless when treated as after-the-fact paperwork.

Frame the practice around what it gives the team — fewer "why did we do this?" conversations later — and let the format choices follow.

## "Decisions" beats "ADRs"

Some teams much prefer "decisions" over the abbreviation "ADRs". When teams use the directory name `decisions/`, something clicks: they start putting more in there — vendor decisions, planning decisions, scheduling decisions — all using the same template.

Our hypothesis: people learn faster from words than abbreviations, the word "record" makes work-in-progress feel forbidden, and some engineers and managers actively dislike the word "architecture". Removing those frictions removes a barrier to writing things down.

## Immutability is an ideal; mutability works in practice

In theory, an accepted ADR should never change — supersede it with a new one if the situation changes. In practice, mutability has worked better for our teams. We add new information into the existing ADR with a date stamp and a note that the information arrived later. This produces a *living document* that everyone can update.

Typical updates: new teammates bringing new context, new offerings appearing, real-world results from running the decision, or third-party changes (vendor capabilities, pricing, license terms). All of these are worth capturing where the decision lives — not in a separate, parallel document.
