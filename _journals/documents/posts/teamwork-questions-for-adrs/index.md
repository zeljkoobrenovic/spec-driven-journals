> Questions a team should answer to agree on how ADRs work in their context: who can create one, what justifies raising or skipping one, the lifecycle, roles, governance, and guiding principles.


The questions below are the ones we've found most useful for getting a team aligned on how ADRs work in their context. Each one comes with an example answer — not a recommendation, just a concrete starting point to push against.

## Who can create an ADR?

Consider specific people, roles, teams, or departments. Also consider who can *commission* an ADR — request one for someone else to author.

**Example.** Anyone in the organization who has read the ADR README page can propose an ADR — meaning they can start writing it and share it with the team.

## What justifies raising an ADR?

Consider the team's ways of working, system structure, cross-team coordination, long-term maintainability, external interfaces, and who you want to benefit.

**Example.** We create an ADR when we want future developers to understand the "why" of what we're doing.

## What justifies *not* raising an ADR?

Consider decisions that aren't about architecture; that are tiny (minimal risk, self-contained, single-developer); that are already covered elsewhere (standards, policies, documentation); or that are temporary (workarounds, proofs of concept, experiments).

**Example.** We skip an ADR when a decision is limited in scope, time, risk, and cost — or is already covered elsewhere.

## What is the lifecycle of an ADR?

Consider creation, research, deciding, implementation, and sunsetting. Consider how to track an ADR through that lifecycle and how to communicate the state to stakeholders.

**Example.** An ADR has five lifecycle stages: Initiating → Researching → Evaluating → Implementing → Maintaining → Sunsetting.

## What are the criteria for moving between lifecycle stages?

Acceptance criteria — how do you know an ADR is good enough to move forward? Is the problem clearly articulated? Have alternatives been considered? Are trade-offs documented? Is all relevant context in place? Have stakeholders been involved? Has feedback been incorporated?

**Example.** An ADR is voted on by stakeholders when the active team has (1) completed their research, (2) completed their evaluation, (3) published the proposal with a one-week request for comments, and (4) addressed all stakeholder comments.

## What roles interact with an ADR?

Consider roles like proposer, researcher, evaluator, reviewer, approver, maintainer. Consider responsibilities for stakeholder communication, publication, maintenance, and periodic review.

**Example.** Each ADR has a primary contact, a secondary contact, and an accountable team. Together they handle communication, publication, maintenance, annual review, and eventual sunsetting.

## How does governance interact with an ADR?

Consider ways of working, compliance needs (legal, HR), and how you handle consensus vs. conflict vs. escalation. Are there people or teams with disproportionate influence — approval, vote, or veto power?

**Example.** Governance authority, in priority order: CEO, CTO, CLO, the implementing team, and the team experts most knowledgeable about the decision. No one else has governance unless explicitly listed in the ADR.

## What principles guide the use of ADRs?

Consider ways of working — moving quickly vs. slowly, consensus vs. conflict, risk vs. safety, public vs. private discussion.

**Example.** We follow these leadership principles: bias for action; disagree-and-commit; 70% estimates are good enough for easily-reversible, easily-isolatable decisions; and public ways of working, with the exception of confidential information as described in the organization's confidentiality agreement.
