> Five areas a team should discuss when adopting ADRs: identifying decisions, making them, enacting and enforcing them, sharing them across projects, and documenting them.

Adopting ADRs is less a tooling change than a team practice. Before picking a format, talk through the five areas below — they shape how ADRs will actually be used.

## Decision identification

- How urgent and how important is this decision?
- Does it have to be made now, or can it wait until more is known?
- Use both personal and collective experience, plus recognized design methods, to spot decisions early.
- Maintain a decision to-do list alongside the product to-do list.

## Decision making

- A range of decision-making techniques exists — both general ones and software-architecture-specific ones (e.g. dialogue mapping).
- Group decision making is an active research topic; don't assume a single technique is universally best.

## Decision enactment and enforcement

- ADs are used in software design and must be communicated to — and accepted by — the stakeholders who fund, develop, and operate the system.
- Architecturally evident coding styles and architecture-focused code reviews are practical ways to enforce decisions in everyday work.
- Revisit ADs during software-evolution work; modernizations frequently surface decisions that need to be re-made.

## Decision sharing (optional)

- Many decisions recur across projects.
- Past experiences — good and bad — are reusable assets when there's an explicit knowledge-management strategy.

## Decision documentation

- Many templates and tools exist for capturing decisions.
- Agile communities favor light formats — e.g. Michael Nygard's ADRs.
- Traditional software engineering offers heavier table-based templates — e.g. IBM UMF, and Tyree and Akerman from Capital One.
