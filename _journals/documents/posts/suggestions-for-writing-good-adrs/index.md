> What makes an ADR good: a clear rationale, a tight focus on one decision, dated entries, and immutable history — plus what belongs in the Context and Consequences sections.

A good ADR is one that a reader who wasn't in the room can still use to understand why a decision was made. A few habits make that more likely.

## Characteristics of a good ADR

- **Rationale.** Explain *why* the decision was made. Include context, the choices considered, pros and cons, feature comparisons, and cost/benefit reasoning where it applies.
- **Specific.** Each ADR is about one decision, not several. Bundling decisions makes them harder to find, harder to supersede, and harder to discuss.
- **Timestamped.** Note when each piece was written. This matters most for content that ages — costs, schedules, scaling assumptions, vendor capabilities.
- **Immutable.** Don't rewrite accepted ADRs. Add new information as an amendment, or supersede the ADR by creating a new one.

## What belongs in the "Context" section

- The organization's situation and business priorities.
- Considerations driven by team makeup — skills, size, distribution, history.
- Pros and cons that are relevant to your goals, described in your own terms rather than as generic feature lists.

## What belongs in the "Consequences" section

- What follows from making this decision — effects, outcomes, follow-ups.
- Any subsequent ADRs the decision triggers. It's common for one big choice to create the need for several smaller ones.
- The after-action review plan. Many teams revisit an ADR roughly a month after acceptance to compare its assumptions against reality and learn from the gap.

## Superseding an ADR

When a new decision replaces or invalidates an older one, create a new ADR and mark the previous one as **Superseded** with a link to the replacement. The history stays intact; the active guidance is clear.
