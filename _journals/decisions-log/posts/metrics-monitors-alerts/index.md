> **Status**: Proposed — research in progress (2020-01-21)
>
> **Decision**: Pending. We are converging on Prometheus + Grafana + Alertmanager for self-hosted metrics and alerting, with ELK for logs, and will revisit a hosted alternative (Datadog or New Relic) if the operational cost of self-hosting outweighs the savings.

## Context

We want metrics, monitoring, and alerting so we know how our applications are performing and so we are paged when they aren't. The market is wide — from venerable open-source tools (Nagios) to fully managed SaaS (Datadog, New Relic) — and we want to anchor on the right combination of capability, cost, and operational burden.

Our default stance is to buy rather than build. We want the chosen tooling to integrate cleanly with our CI/CD pipeline and our deployment clouds.

## Decision

We have not yet committed. Current direction:

- **Metrics and alerting:** Prometheus + Alertmanager, visualized via Grafana.
- **Logs:** ELK (Elasticsearch + Logstash + Kibana), or Graylog as a lighter-weight alternative.
- **On-call routing:** PagerDuty or VictorOps when we formalize an on-call rotation.
- **Fallback:** if the operational burden of self-hosted Prometheus/ELK exceeds the savings, we switch to Datadog or New Relic.

We are starting with the plausible ends of the spectrum to calibrate: the oldest, simplest, free tool (Nagios) and the newest, most featureful, paid tool (New Relic). The final choice will sit between them.

## Rationale

1. **Prometheus + Grafana is the de facto open-source standard.** It is well supported in the cloud-native ecosystem, integrates with the platforms we are likely to run on, and has a healthy plug-in surface for alerting.
2. **Alert on user-visible conditions, not infrastructure causes.** Page-load time and successful-request rate are good alert signals; CPU utilization and per-process metrics are diagnostic, not alerting, signals. The tooling needs to support this discipline.
3. **Build vs. buy is a volume question.** Hosted services are easier and cheaper at small volumes. At very high request volumes the per-metric or per-host pricing of SaaS can dominate. We will revisit when our volumes are known.
4. **Self-hosted ELK requires ongoing care.** Elasticsearch operations are not zero-cost. If our log volumes turn out to be modest, a hosted log service may be cheaper in total.
5. **Avoid running our own paging.** Whatever we pick for metrics and logs, we will use a hosted notification system (PagerDuty / VictorOps / OpsGenie). Running a paging system in-house is rarely worth the effort.

## Implications

1. **Operational ownership.** Choosing self-hosted Prometheus + ELK means someone owns running them. We will budget for that explicitly.
2. **Logging philosophy.** We will prefer structured event logs (with consistent attributes like `userid`, `sessionid`) over freeform text where the application generates them. Freeform OS-level logs still matter for hardware-level issues.
3. **Long-term storage.** Prometheus is not built for long retention. If we want long history we will add Thanos, Cortex, or an external TSDB (e.g. InfluxDB, TimescaleDB) and ingest from there.
4. **Cardinality discipline.** Both Prometheus and most SaaS billing models punish high-cardinality labels. We will set conventions early to keep label sets bounded.
5. **Reversible.** Telemetry plumbing is contained; switching backends is feasible if the chosen stack proves wrong.

## Conclusion

The Prometheus / Grafana / Alertmanager / ELK direction is the most defensible starting point given today's information. We will firm it up once we have a clearer picture of request volumes, log volumes, and the operational headcount we can dedicate to running our own monitoring stack.

## Notes — community stack patterns surveyed

The catalogue below captures stacks we observed in the wider community while researching this decision. It is not prescriptive — it informs our short list.

### Themes

- **Open-source pure-play:** Prometheus + Grafana + Alertmanager, with ELK or Graylog for logs.
- **Hybrid:** self-hosted Prometheus for metrics, hosted Sentry or Rollbar for error reporting, PagerDuty for paging.
- **Fully hosted:** Datadog or New Relic for everything, with PagerDuty for alerts.
- **Legacy:** Nagios + Nagiosgraph remains common in established environments; Zabbix has strong proponents for "everything in one tool" deployments.

### Recurring observations

- **Prometheus alerting requires tuning.** It is excellent once tuned. Alert on end-user-visible conditions (page load, request success) rather than CPU or memory.
- **AWS managed Elasticsearch has been flaky in others' experience.** Self-running Elasticsearch on instances is a common workaround.
- **CloudWatch metrics get expensive** — many teams limit them to infrastructure-level signals only.
- **Kafka as a log buffer** is valuable at scale (it absorbs traffic spikes that would crush Elasticsearch) but adds significant operational complexity.
- **Loki** (Grafana Labs) is worth watching as a Prometheus-style log aggregator that may simplify the logging side of the stack.
- **Datadog** is universally easy to set up and universally expensive at scale.
- **Synthetic / external monitoring** (Checkly, Site24x7, StatusCake, Healthchecks.io) complements but does not replace internal metrics.

### Concrete stacks observed in the wild

For reference, common combinations reported by practitioners:

- Prometheus + Grafana + Alertmanager + ELK
- Prometheus + Grafana + Alertmanager + Splunk + PagerDuty
- Telegraf + Prometheus + InfluxDB + Grafana
- Datadog + Sentry + PagerDuty
- Datadog + Prometheus + Grafana
- Sensu + Graphite + ELK
- icinga2 + VictorOps + New Relic + Sentry + Slack
- AppDynamics + Papertrail + PagerDuty + Healthchecks.io + Stackdriver
- Sematext (metrics + logs + traces + RUM, single vendor)
- Zabbix (single tool, with custom scripts)
- Azure Monitor / Log Analytics / App Insights + PagerDuty (Azure-only shops)
- Wavefront + Scalyr + PagerDuty + Stackstorm + Slack

The pattern across all of them is consistent: a metrics layer, a logs layer, an alerting/routing layer, and (often) an exception-tracking layer. The differences are about cost model, operational burden, and how many of those layers the team is willing to run themselves.
