> **Status**: Accepted (2020-01-21)
>
> **Decision**: We will adopt high-trust teamwork as a core value and operating principle of the team. The commitment is supported by explicit principles, regular feedback mechanisms, and a defined process for addressing trust breaches when they occur.

## Context

How a team handles trust shapes almost everything else about how it works — speed of decisions, quality of disagreement, willingness to surface bad news, and the cost of coordination. We have been operating without an explicit stance on this, which means the trust level varies by sub-team and by relationship and is fragile to changes in membership.

We considered three broad postures:

- **Low- or no-trust teamwork**, relying on controls, sign-offs, and surveillance.
- **High-trust teamwork**, relying on shared principles, transparency, and direct conversation.
- **Adjacent frameworks** such as cognitive-diversity teams, centralized decision-making, and Agile team structures — which address how a team is composed or governed, but not the underlying trust posture.

High-trust teamwork is the posture most aligned with the kind of organization we want to be and with the work we are actually doing, which depends on fast, candid collaboration more than on tight control.

## Decision

We adopt high-trust teamwork as a stated value of the team. In practice that means:

- Transparency, honesty, integrity, and respect are explicit, named expectations of everyone on the team.
- Decisions and reasoning are shared by default, not on request.
- Disagreement is welcome and expected; silence is treated as a problem, not as consent.
- Trust breaches are addressed directly through a named process rather than allowed to fester.

## Rationale

1. **Better collaboration and decisions.** When people trust each other, information moves faster, dissent surfaces sooner, and decisions get made with the full picture in the room.
2. **Higher engagement and retention.** Working in a high-trust environment is consistently linked to motivation, satisfaction, and people staying.
3. **More room for innovation.** Trust lowers the personal cost of proposing something risky or unfinished, which is exactly the kind of contribution that compounds over time.
4. **Lower coordination overhead.** Heavyweight controls exist to compensate for missing trust; with trust in place, those controls become unnecessary friction.
5. **Coherent with how we already want to work.** High trust complements and amplifies adjacent practices (Agile, cross-functional teams, distributed work); the alternatives would undercut them.

## Implications

1. **Principles, written down.** We will articulate what high trust means for us — transparency, honesty, integrity, respect — in concrete behaviors, not slogans.
2. **Feedback loops.** Regular feedback channels (1:1s, retrospectives, anonymous routes where needed) will be in place so trust signals are visible and actionable.
3. **Reinforcement.** Behaviors that build trust will be recognized openly; behaviors that erode it will be named and addressed.
4. **Breach process.** When trust is broken — through miscommunication, mistake, or otherwise — we will use a defined, low-drama process to repair it rather than relying on improvisation.
5. **Onboarding.** New team members will learn the principles and the breach process as part of joining, not by osmosis.

## Conclusion

High-trust teamwork is a deliberate choice with real implications: it asks more of individuals in candor and integrity, and less of the organization in controls and oversight. We are committing to the principles, the practices that support them, and the conversations that will inevitably be needed to keep them honest.
