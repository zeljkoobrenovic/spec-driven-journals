> **Status**: Accepted (2020-01-21)
>
> **Decision**: We will use Docker Swarm as our container orchestration tool for managing our microservices across a cluster of nodes.

## Context

We are moving to a microservices architecture and need a way to deploy, scale, and operate containers across a cluster. We evaluated Kubernetes, Docker Swarm, and Mesosphere DC/OS. Our teams already use Docker locally and in CI, and the workloads we need to orchestrate today do not require the more advanced features of Kubernetes or DC/OS. Operational simplicity and a shallow learning curve are higher priorities than capability ceiling.

## Decision

We will adopt Docker Swarm as our container orchestration platform. It will manage deployment, scaling, load balancing, and availability of our containerized services.

## Rationale

1. **Simplicity.** Swarm follows the same model as Docker itself, so engineers familiar with Docker pick it up quickly. There is no separate mental model to learn.
2. **Integration with existing tooling.** Swarm works seamlessly with Docker CLI and Docker Compose, letting us reuse our existing definitions and workflows.
3. **Built-in load balancing.** Swarm distributes traffic across service replicas out of the box, so we do not need a separate load-balancing layer for east-west traffic.
4. **Easy horizontal scaling.** Adding or removing nodes from the cluster is straightforward, and Swarm rebalances services automatically.
5. **High availability.** Swarm spreads services across nodes and reschedules tasks on node failure, giving us a usable HA baseline without extra components.

## Implications

1. **Feature ceiling.** Swarm lacks some of the advanced features available in Kubernetes — fine-grained autoscaling, richer self-healing, and a deeper ecosystem of operators. If we hit those limits, we will need to migrate.
2. **Coupling to Docker.** Swarm is tightly coupled to the Docker stack. Moving away from Docker later would also mean moving away from Swarm.
3. **Smaller ecosystem.** Compared to Kubernetes, Swarm has fewer third-party integrations and a smaller community, so we will sometimes have to build or self-host things we could otherwise pick off the shelf.
4. **Migration path.** We will keep an eye on growth in our orchestration needs and treat a future migration to Kubernetes as a known-possible follow-up rather than a surprise.

## Conclusion

For our current scale and team experience, Docker Swarm gives us most of the value of a full orchestrator at a fraction of the operational cost. We accept the feature-ceiling trade-off in exchange for simplicity now, and will revisit the choice if our needs outgrow what Swarm offers.
