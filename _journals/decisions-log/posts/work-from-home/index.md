> **Status**: Accepted (2020-01-21)
>
> **Decision**: We will adopt a permanent hybrid work-from-home policy for eligible full-time employees. Eligible staff may work remotely up to three days per week, with at least two days on-site. The policy will be reviewed periodically and adjusted as needed.

## Context

During the COVID-19 pandemic most of the company shifted to remote work as a necessity. Through that period we observed real benefits — increased flexibility, better work-life balance, reduced commuting, and no measurable drop in delivery — and many employees have explicitly asked to keep working from home in some form after the pandemic.

We now need to decide whether to revert to full on-site work, formalize remote work as a permanent option, or land somewhere in between. The team has demonstrated it can operate effectively remotely; the question is how to make that sustainable rather than provisional.

## Decision

We will introduce a permanent hybrid work policy with the following shape:

- All full-time employees whose roles can be performed remotely, and who have demonstrated effective remote work during the pandemic, are eligible.
- Eligible employees may work from home up to **three days per week**.
- At least **two days per week** are spent in the office to maintain in-person collaboration.
- The company provides the equipment and tools needed to work effectively from home.
- Regular check-ins between team members and managers are expected, not optional.
- The policy is reviewed periodically and adjusted based on outcomes.

## Rationale

1. **The evidence is in.** Two years of forced experimentation has shown the team can deliver effectively from home; we are now choosing on the basis of data, not speculation.
2. **Employee demand.** A large share of the team has explicitly asked for the option to continue remote work. Acting on that signal supports retention and engagement.
3. **Talent and retention.** A formal hybrid policy widens the candidate pool geographically and removes a common reason for high-performers to leave for competitors offering flexibility.
4. **Balance of flexibility and cohesion.** Three remote days respect the productivity and life benefits of remote work; two in-office days preserve the spontaneous interaction and team identity that fully distributed work erodes.
5. **Aligned with company values.** The policy is consistent with our stated commitments to employee well-being and a healthy, sustainable working environment.

## Implications

1. **Eligibility and fairness.** Managers will need clear guidance on which roles qualify and how to handle edge cases, so the policy does not become arbitrary or perceived as unfair.
2. **Equipment and tooling.** Home-office equipment, security tooling, and collaboration software become standing operational concerns, not one-off pandemic spend.
3. **Management practice.** Managers will be coached on running hybrid teams: making meetings work for in-person and remote attendees, measuring output, and avoiding a two-tier culture.
4. **Office use.** Office space, desk booking, and on-site days may need to shift to support the new pattern rather than assume daily attendance.
5. **Review.** We will review satisfaction, performance, retention, and collaboration health at defined intervals and adjust the policy if it is not delivering.

## Conclusion

A permanent hybrid policy is the choice that matches both the evidence we have and the kind of company we want to be. It commits to flexibility as a default rather than a concession, while keeping enough in-person time to preserve the team's cohesion and culture.
