> **Status**: Accepted (2020-01-21)
>
> **Decision**: We will adopt Agile as our team's software development methodology, replacing the current plan-driven workflow. The change will be supported by targeted training, explicit process documentation, and KPIs to evaluate effectiveness.

## Context

Our current workflow is plan-driven: requirements are largely fixed up front, work is delivered in long cycles, and changes mid-flight are expensive. This has become a poor fit for the rate at which our market, customers, and product priorities are shifting.

In recent retrospectives the team has consistently raised three problems: slow time-to-market, limited room to incorporate feedback once a project is underway, and friction collaborating across disciplines. Agile methodologies — iterative delivery, frequent communication, embracing change — are explicitly designed around these tensions, and there is enough industry evidence and team appetite to commit to the move rather than experiment indefinitely.

## Decision

We will adopt Agile as the default methodology for the team. Specifically:

- Work is planned and delivered in short iterations with a working increment at the end of each.
- Requirements are treated as a living backlog, reprioritized regularly rather than fixed at project start.
- The team commits to a small set of Agile ceremonies (planning, review, retrospective) and adjusts the cadence based on what works.
- Success is measured against agreed KPIs rather than against adherence to a specific framework.

## Rationale

1. **Iterative delivery reduces risk.** Shipping in short cycles surfaces problems early, when they are still cheap to fix, instead of at the end of a long project.
2. **Built for changing requirements.** Agile assumes priorities will change and provides explicit mechanisms (backlog, reprioritization, increments) for absorbing that change.
3. **Stronger cross-functional collaboration.** Regular ceremonies and a shared backlog pull product, engineering, and stakeholders into the same conversation rather than handing artifacts over a wall.
4. **Faster time-to-market.** Delivering working software incrementally lets us put value in front of customers sooner and learn from real usage.
5. **Aligned with team appetite.** The team has asked for this; adoption will land better than an imposed process change.

## Implications

1. **Training and ramp-up.** We will assess current familiarity with Agile, then run targeted training and pair newcomers with more experienced practitioners.
2. **Process documentation.** Planning cadence, definition of done, backlog hygiene, and ceremony formats will be written down so the practice is repeatable and reviewable.
3. **KPIs.** We will define a small set of indicators — for example cycle time, predictability of delivery, defect escape rate, and team health — to track whether Agile is actually delivering the expected benefits.
4. **Stakeholder communication.** Stakeholders accustomed to long-horizon roadmaps will need to be brought into the iterative model, including how scope, dates, and commitments are now communicated.
5. **Honest review.** After a defined period we will evaluate the KPIs and either continue, adjust the practice, or pull back where Agile has not improved outcomes.

## Conclusion

Adopting Agile is a deliberate response to slow cycles, poor adaptability, and weak collaboration in our current process. We expect it to improve both how we deliver and how we work together, and we are committing to measuring that rather than assuming it.
