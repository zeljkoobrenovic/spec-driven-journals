> **Status**: Accepted (2020-01-21)
>
> **Decision**: We will use Google Cloud Platform (GCP) as the cloud infrastructure for our application, standardising on Compute Engine, Cloud Storage, Cloud SQL, and Firebase.

## Context

We need a cloud platform to host our application that gives us computing, storage, networking, and managed data services without forcing us to operate the underlying infrastructure. We are working under a constrained budget and expect traffic to grow unevenly, so cost, elasticity, and operational simplicity matter more than feature-by-feature parity with other providers.

## Decision

We will adopt GCP as the cloud infrastructure for our application. The initial service selection is:

- **Compute Engine** for virtual machines and general compute.
- **Cloud Storage** for object storage and file hosting.
- **Cloud SQL** for managed relational databases.
- **Firebase** for application development and hosting concerns where it is a good fit.

## Rationale

1. **Cost-effectiveness.** GCP's pricing and sustained-use discounts work well for our usage profile and budget constraints.
2. **Scalability.** Compute Engine and the surrounding managed services scale to absorb traffic spikes without infrastructure rework on our side.
3. **Reliability.** GCP's managed services come with built-in backups, replication, and disaster-recovery capabilities, reducing the operational burden of running these ourselves.
4. **Flexibility.** GCP spans compute, storage, data, AI, and analytics, so we can extend into new capability areas on the same platform rather than stitching together vendors.

## Implications

1. **Training.** Engineering and operations teams will need ramp-up on GCP services and tooling.
2. **Re-architecture.** Parts of the application will need adjustments to fit the selected managed services cleanly.
3. **Infrastructure code.** Provisioning, deployment, and infrastructure-as-code artefacts will be updated to target GCP.
4. **Ongoing cost management.** Pay-as-you-go pricing means we will need ongoing monitoring of usage and resource right-sizing to keep costs in line with budget.
5. **Vendor concentration.** Standardizing on GCP creates a stronger dependency on a single provider; we will be mindful of portability for workloads where preserving it is cheap.

## Conclusion

GCP gives us a cost-effective, scalable, and reliable foundation with enough breadth to grow into. With a thoughtful migration and ongoing cost discipline, we expect it to deliver a robust hosting environment without locking us into operational complexity.
